Do It!

Your challenge is to analyze facts and data on climate change and use your findings to make a presentation to suggest things we can do to help. You need to present the proof and results accurately if you are going to be convincing. Then, make suggestions for what we can do to help.

Steps to Follow

Use NASA's Climate Kids website to find the articles, pictures, and other resources needed for your presentation.

  1. Research global climate change. Make sure you understand what it is. Read some of the Big Questions articles. In your ePortfolio, list which ones you read and your major takeaway from each. Here are a few recommended articles to choose from:
    • What is global climate change?
    • What is the big deal with carbon?
    • What is the greenhouse effect?
    • What can we do to help?
  2. Research the proof. How do we know that the climate is changing? In your ePortfolio, list the resources you review and your major takeaway from each. Here are a few suggestions to choose from:
    • The Climate Time Machine (You may want to take screenshots of what you see in this interactive activity.)
    • How do we know the climate is changing?
    • What is happening in the ocean?
    • What is ocean acidification?
    • Lemur moms
    • Birds and climate change
    • What can trees tell us about climate change?
  3. Choose a topic that interests you. Then, explore some of the other articles, interviews, and games that give you more information on your topic. Again, in your ePortfolio, list the resources you review and your major takeaway from each.
  4. Find pictures, charts, videos, or other multimedia to help prove your point. You can also take screenshots of games or videos.
    HINT: Put "gallery" into the search bar and see what shows up on the Climate Kids website!
  5. Research what we can do to help. You can find ideas in articles, games, interviews, and more.
  6. Prepare your presentation.
    1. Read the tips in the What You Should Know section.
    2. Outline your argument using the example outline in the What You Should Know section (or create your own). 
    3. Use PowerPoint or another presentation software to organize and show your visuals.
  7. Practice your presentation in front of friends or family. Ask for tips on how to improve.
  8. Present your argument to your class.
