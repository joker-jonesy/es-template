Taking Care of Snap Circuits

Each Snap Circuits piece has electrical components to make it work in your circuit. These pieces can be damaged if they're not taken care of properly. Use the tips below to prevent your pieces from being broken.

Attaching Pieces to the Grid

  1. Set the Snap Circuits piece on the grid and line up the snaps with the pegs on the grid, or the snaps of another piece.
  2. Put a finger on each of the snaps on the top piece and push evenly.

Removing Pieces from the Grid

  1. Place two fingers on each side of the Snap Circuits piece.
  2. Carefully lift the piece directly up.
  3. If the piece is too hard to remove this way, tilt it sideways to remove.
  4. When removing the battery holder, tilt the holder towards the snaps to remove.

  

Electricity Review

Electricity is moving electrons. All circuits work the same basic way. Electricity flows from a power source along a wiring path to an electrical device (load), and then back to the power source. 

Materials that conduct electricity are called conductors. 

Materials that do not conduct electricity are called insulators.

How Does a Light Bulb Work?

Take a close look at a type of light bulb called the incandescent light bulb. These are the traditional light bulbs widely used today. 

Light Bulbs Depend on Resistance

Light bulbs depend on an electrical principle called resistance:

As you have learned, electricity is electrons moving from one atom to another. Electrons move easily along the copper electrical wire that typically connects the light to a power source, like a wall outlet or battery.

The electricity enters the light bulb through either the bottom or side of the of the screw-in base. It travels up a wire inside of the bulb.

Next, the electricity passes through a part of the bulb called the filament. The filament is a very long, very thin coil of wire. It is usually made of a type of metal called tungsten. The electricity continues down the other wire and out the base of the bulb. 

What happens in the filament is actually what makes a light bulb so useful. 

The filament is so thin (about one-hundredth of an inch) that the electrons are constantly bumping into atoms as they move around. All that bumping around creates enough
heat that the filament begins to glow. This is the light we see. When the filament uses up electrical energy and converts it to heat and light, this is the concept of resistance.

Resistance Is Important  

Resistance is very important in electrical circuits. Here is a good way to better understand how resistance works in the filament. Rub your hands together quickly. Do you feel them getting warm?

Rubbing your hands together changes kinetic energy  (the energy of motion) into heat. The filament works in a similar way. It heats up and glows when the moving electrons rub up against each other as they move through.

Digging Deeper

Who do you think invented the light bulb - Thomas Edison or Sir Humphrey Davy?

Most people credit Thomas Edison with inventing the light bulb. Did you know that it was actually invented by British scientist, Sir Humphrey Davy, in 1802? Thomas Edison invented the first long-lasting light bulb in 1880. Edison’s light bulb was not the first, but it was his light bulb that worked well enough for people to use in their daily lives.
