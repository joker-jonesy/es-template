Do It! Energy Efficiency: Keep Our SmartLab Cool

 The Challenge

Electronics create heat and must be cooled down to prevent damage from overheating. Your challenge is to measure how much heat is produced by the electronics in your classroom. Use temperature sensors to measure and record the temperatures to the nearest hundredth. Compare the temperatures to determine which gives off the most heat. How can you save energy and reduce the demand for air conditioning in your classroom? Develop ideas to help lower the temperature of your classroom environment and reduce future demand for energy to cool your classroom. Create a presentation to share with your principal.

  2. Presentation

Collect Temperature Data

Collect temperature data of electronics in a room using the Vernier Surface Thermometer or the Vernier Go!Temp sensor. The Vernier Surface Temperature and Vernier Go!Temp sensors are both electronic temperature sensors that plug into your computer, and collect and graph temperature data. The screen displays the temperatures as they are collected:

You can watch the temperature change in the ‘Temperature’ box on the bottom left or by watching the graph. The program will automatically populate the temperature table as it records the data.

Begin by finding the temperatures of a computer that has been on for a while, one that is in sleep mode, and one that has not yet been turned on. These items are listed in the left column of the table. Use the temperature sensor to measure the temperature of each in degrees Fahrenheit. If the temperature given is written in a decimal value, round it to the nearest hundredth and record it in the right column of the table.

Find three to five other electronic devices in the classroom or lab to measure temperature. List the items in the left column and their temperatures in the right column.

Use your data to answer the following questions:

Which device in your classroom gives off the most heat?

Do all of the computers have the same temperature? What is the temperature of each device?

Look back at the temperatures recorded for the computers that are off, in sleep mode, or running.

Which temperature is the greatest?

Which is the least?

How do you know?

Use your data to write five comparison sentences. For example, if the temperature recorded for an iPad is 97.56° and the temperature for a computer while on is recorded as 114.5°, then you could write:

The iPad’s temperature is less than the computer’s temperature when it’s on because 97.56° < 114.5°.

Remember that when comparing decimals, you first look to the digit that is in the greatest place value. If it is the same in both numbers, then move one place value to the right and compare those numbers.  Be sure to use the <  or > symbols, as in the example above, to support your comparison sentences.

After writing your five comparison sentences, put the values of the temperatures for all the electronics tested in order from least to greatest.

  • What does this information tell you? How do you know?
  • What patterns do you notice? Do they make sense?
  • How will you use this information to help find suggestions for saving energy?
  • How will you use this information in your letter or presentation?

Presentation

Write a letter or create a presentation for your principal. It should accomplish the following:

  • Teach your principal about sustainability
  • Make suggestions on how to keep the school cool and the electronics safe without turning up the air conditioner

Be sure to include the following:

  • Screenshots of your work
  • Your data, graphs, and other experimental results
  • Photos or videos of your experiment in action
  • What you learned from the experiment(s)
  • Your recommendations to decrease the heat created by electronics
