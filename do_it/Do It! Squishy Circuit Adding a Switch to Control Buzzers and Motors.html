Do It! Squishy Circuits: Explore Switches and 2D Shapes

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to make a design that incorporates a buzzer or motor, a switch, and additional resistors using Squishy Circuits. The design needs to include 2D shapes. You can design something abstract from your imagination that has these components in various locations. Or, you can create a geometric object from real life, such as a building or a fire truck, which may have specific places where it would make sense to put a motor, buzzer, light and/or switch. What do you want to make?

 Project Steps

  1. Solve Shape Riddles

  2. Sketch Your Design

  3. Build Your Design

Solve Shape Riddles

To help you get started, use the clues below to determine which shape is being described. Each clue highlights particular attributes of two-dimensional shapes. Take note of what the attributes for each are, as you will be including these shapes in your project!

Sketch Your Design

  1. Sketch a design that is made up of 2D shapes. It’s your choice whether you design something abstract or an object from real life. Use the following criteria:
    1. Your design should include at least 6 shapes.
    2. There should be at least one of each of the following shapes in your design: a right triangle, a parallelogram, a rectangle, a rhombus, and a trapezoid.
    3. Use a different color of your choosing to outline each of the different shapes in your design.  For example, all right, isosceles triangles could be outlined in blue and all trapezoids could be outlined in red.
  2. Include a key at the bottom of your drawing that includes the following:
    1. The name of each of the shapes you included. Be very specific when labeling the shapes. For example, ‘right, isosceles triangle’.
    2. Include a sketch of each of the shapes used in your design using the same color as you used in your design. Use the color to highlight the different attributes that define the shape. (See the image below for examples.) For example, if you chose to use blue to draw right, isosceles triangles in your design, then use a blue marker to trace the sides that are equal (the same length) and the right angle in your key drawing and label. If you chose to use red to draw trapezoids in your design, then use a red marker to trace the pair of parallel sides in your key drawing and label.
    3. Use the shape riddles if you need help identifying the important attributes.
  3. Look at your sketch and determine where you can add a buzzer or a motor; you must include at least one. For example, if you created a house, could you add a light or a doorbell to your design? Where would you add it? How would you build the shape to incorporate the circuit?
  4. Add the buzzer or motor to your sketch, circling it so that it can clearly be seen in your drawing.

Build Your Design

Create your design using the Squishy Circuits Use the dough to build both the shapes and the circuit. The steps below will guide you through building the circuit.

Add a Buzzer or Motor Add a Switch Combination Circuit
  1. Use two pieces of conductive dough to make the shape that you will be adding a buzzer or motor to.
  2. Build the basic closed circuit on the shape in your design.
  3. Instead of an LED, use a motor or buzzer for your resistor.
  4. Switch the circuit on using the battery pack.
  5. Switch the circuit off using the battery pack when finished.

Add a switch to your closed circuit so you can control the flow of electricity to your motor or buzzer. This will allow you to turn on the buzzer or motor without switching on and off the battery pack each time.

The official name for the switch included in the Squishy Circuits kit is called a Single Pole Single Throw Switch (SPST). It is named after its job, which is to switch a single circuit on and off.

A combination circuit can have multiple resistors. Try adding additional LEDs or another resistor to your closed circuit. What happens to the resistors when you turn the switch on? Or, if you have time, create a separate combination circuit with multiple resistors. Check out this Rocket or Submarine on the Squishy Circuits website for inspiration.
