Do It! Snap Circuits Green Energy: Solar Energy and Solar Panels

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to design, build, and test electrical circuits that use the solar cell. You will produce and store energy using solar power. Then, test to see if the energy you produce can power a motor, lights, and more. After testing your solar model, you will apply the concept of solar cells to solar panels. You will design a roof and draw a plan for covering the roof with solar panels.

 Getting Started

To succeed in your challenge, you will want to complete some introductory projects first. These experiences will give you the skills and experience needed to build and teach others about your final challenge projects.

For all of your projects, remember to:

  • Record your work.
  • Take photographs.
  • Label images.
  • Write about what you did and what happened.

No Sun? No Problem! Try shining a lamp on your solar cell.

Build the Introductory Projects

These introductory projects will give you the experience and knowledge needed to succeed in your challenge. You will experiment with the best angle for your light source, how different circuits generate different amounts of voltage, and what this means for the operation of devices. 

Snap Circuits Green Energy Instruction Manual

  • #3 Best Charging Circuits - What is the best angle for your light source?
  • #4 Solar Power - Why does the voltage drop when you connect the yellow LED?
  • #5 Solar Motor - In circuit #4, replace the yellow LED (D5) with the motor (M4, facing either direction) and place the wind fan on it. Now press the switch. How does the voltage change as the solar cell runs the fan?

Build Advanced Circuits or Design and Build Your Own

Now you are ready for a greater challenge. Complete at least two advanced projects or try creating your own circuit design and running it with your solar cell.

Are you able to make your project work even when the solar cell is not collecting solar power?

  • #6 Solar Charger - Find the best position of the solar cell to make the highest current.
  • #7 Solar Charger 5mA - What happens when you cover the solar cell with your hand?
  • #13 Light Charger - What does it mean when the red LED lights up?

           

LED bulbs only light up when electricity flows through the circuit in the right direction. Carefully build each circuit following the directions. If the LED does not light up, try turning it around. Use the + sign to help you place your LED correctly.

Draw Your Roof and Label the Dimensions

Now that you have explored solar energy, apply the concept of solar cells to solar panels by considering the most efficient way to cover a roof with solar panels. Your roof will have an area of 200 square feet. Each solar panel will be 4 by 6 feet. Think about the possible dimensions and choose the dimensions you will use. Remember that you want to have room for as many solar panels as possible.

You will draw your roof using graph paper or using Geogebra. Think about how many feet each square will represent. Label the dimensions of each side.

To draw using Geogebra:

  1. Click on the Polygon tool from the toolbar at the top. It is shaped like a triangle.
  2. Click the point on the grid where you want to make a vertex for the shape you are drawing. To make a rectangle, you will click on 4 points and then back to your first point to complete the shape.
  3. Click on the arrow from the toolbar if you want to move the points to change the size of the shape or location of the corners.
  4. Click on the a = 2 button and select ABC Text to label your sides.
  5. The toolbar in the upper right hand corner (represented by a circle and triangle overlapping three lines) can be used to change the color.

Draw Each Solar Panel and Label the Dimensions

Each solar panel will be 4 by 6 feet. Think about how to place your panels to fit as many on the roof as possible. Once you have a good design, sketch each solar panel. Label each panel with the dimensions and the area.

Find the perimeter of the roof and of a single panel. Find the total area of all of your solar panels by adding up the areas of each solar panel.
