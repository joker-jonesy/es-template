Do It! Snap Circuits Green Energy: Liquid Battery Data Collection

 The Challenge

To make sure we do not run out of energy, we need to find and use renewable or alternative energy sources. 

The energy produced by alternative energy sources (like wind or solar) is most often converted directly into electricity. If this energy is not immediately used while it is being produced, it needs to be stored so we can use it later.

Batteries help store electricity, allowing us to collect renewable energy and use it at more convenient times. Here you will explore batteries and how they release their stored energy.

Your challenge is to design, build, and test electrical circuits that are powered by a liquid battery. Then, you will record how long each liquid battery works and round each to the nearest hundredth of a second.

 Getting Started

  1. Is your battery assembled? If not, turn to page 4 in the manual for instructions.
  2. Do you have water, salt, juice, and/or soda? To do activities in this challenge, you need these resources.
  3. Locate the jumper wires. The liquid battery connects to circuitry parts with jumper wires.
  4. You will need a sink and paper towels to wash and dry the battery when you are done.

TIPS

  • Gently stir the liquid in the cells about once every minute.
  • Do not drink the liquids used in this experiment!
  • The LEDs are dim. You may need to work in a dark place to see the lights turn on and off. LED bulbs only light up when electricity flows through the circuit in the right direction. Carefully build each circuit following the directions. If the LED does not light up, try turning it around. Use the + sign to help you place your LED correctly.

Introductory Projects

The introductory projects in the Snap Circuits Green Energy Instruction Manual will give you the experience and knowledge needed to succeed in your challenge. You will test how much electricity the liquid power source produces with different types of liquids. Which liquid do you think produces more energy: soda or juice?

  • #62 Liquid Battery - Construct a liquid battery using cola.
  • #63 Juice Battery - Replace the soda in the liquid energy source with fruit juice. Sour-tasting juices like lemon or grapefruit work best. Measure the voltage and current for your juice battery like you did with the soda. Try different juices and compare them. 
  • #64 Cola Light - Test LED lights using your cola liquid battery.

  • #65 Yellow Cola - Replace the red LED (D1) with the yellow LED (D5). Compare the LED brightness and voltage change to the red LED in the previous project. The liquid energy source does not produce enough electricity to run the W1 horn or M4 motor.  

With each project: 

  1. Test each liquid battery three times. 
  2. Record how long each liquid battery works and round each to the nearest hundredth of a second.
  3. Put the results into the Liquid Battery Test Table. Be sure to include the units for your time measurements.

Recording this data allows an individual to compare the effectiveness of each battery.
