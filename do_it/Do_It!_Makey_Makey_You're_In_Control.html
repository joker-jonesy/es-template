Do It! Makey Makey: Music and the Coordinate Plane

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to make a programmable circuit that produces sound or music using Makey Makey software and objects that conduct electricity.

This is your opportunity to play the piano on a banana or to make the computer ring a cowbell. There is a project idea for everyone. You just have to use your creativity to find materials and program your software to understand your commands.

Then, explore how you can use your sounds to create patterns, and how these patterns make ordered pairs on a coordinate plane.

  • First, you will need to know which materials conduct electricity and find them in your kit or in the SmartLab.
  • Then, you will need to think of a project idea. The Makey Makey Gallery has lots of projects by artists, students, and just about anyone. You can even add your project to the online collection.
  • Last, you will set up the Makey Makey software with your computer and start your project.

 Getting Started

Turn Makey Makey into a Mouse

Are you ready to try Makey Makey? Start by turning your Makey Makey into a mouse and keyboard for your computer.

  1. Open a word processing document and type, “Hello World.”
  2. Plug the USB cable into your computer and into the Makey Makey board; close the Popup window if it appears on your computer screen.
  3. Attach one alligator clip to the Earth holes; attach another alligator clip to the Space pad.

 

Check Your Connections

The circuit must be complete to send electrical signals. Hold the free ends of the alligator clips in your fingers. Tap the tip of the alligator clip that runs to the Space pad and watch what happens to the cursor on the word processing document.

Create Your Instrument

Now that you have used Makey Makey to turn something into drums, consider what other type of unique instrument you could make. What sounds would it make? Would it have strings or buttons? Would you hold it, hang it, or would it lay on the table or ground?

  1. With the materials available to you, sketch out a design of what your instrument might look like.
  2. Consider what will you attach the clips to (some options include paper fasteners, fruit, or other conductive material).
  3. Consider where the connections are going to be placed on your instrument.
    • Do they need to be close to each other so you can reach them?
    • How do the wires affect how you play your instrument?
    • What other considerations should you address?
  4. Make your instrument using Makey Makey and your computer.

Make Sounds in Scratch

Now that you have your instrument, you can program Scratch to make the sounds.

Start by learning how to use Makey Makey with Scratch. Follow along with the video in section 1 of the lesson.

Create sounds in Scratch. Read section 3 of the lesson instructions and scroll through the visual instructions at the bottom of the section on the Makey Makey website to see how to complete this. Plan for your instrument to make at least two different sounds or notes. You can use traditional music sounds like the sound of a piano and the sound of a drum, or you could use sounds like a cat’s meow and a train horn.

Test your code and your instrument to see if they both work.

Optional: Try making up a song or playing a simple song you know!

Chart Your Pattern

Now that you have a working instrument, explore how you can use its sounds to create a unique pattern. 

You will play your sounds in numerical patterns. You have choices on how to do this. You’ll start by charting two numerical patterns. Here is an example:

Pattern 1 0 1 2 3
Pattern 2 0 3 6 9

Pattern 1 uses the rule, “Add 1.” Pattern 2 uses the rule, “Add 3.”

Option 1: The numerical patterns could show how many times to play a certain sound.

Let’s say that the sound associated with pattern 1 is a drum beat and pattern 2 is a piano sound.

With this pattern, it would sound like 1 drum beat, 3 piano sounds, 2 drum beats, 6 piano sounds, 3 drum beats, 9 piano sounds, etc.

Option 2: Another option would be to use the numerical pattern to show how long each sound is activated.

Using the same pattern as above, it would have a 1 second drum beat, a 3 second piano sound, 2 second drum beat, 6 seconds piano sound, 3 second drum beat, 9 second piano sound, etc.

Can you think of another way to interpret the pattern above?

Once you decide on what the pattern represents (length of note, number of times the sound is heard, etc.), you can label your chart appropriately.

Graph Your Pattern

Use your rule to make ordered pairs and graph them on a coordinate plane. You can use graphing paper or a software program like PDF Filler.

For example, if you were plotting the patterns above, you would plot the following coordinate pairs: (0, 0), (1, 3), (2, 6), and (3, 9). Your graph would look like this:

If you use PDF Filler:

  1. Use this link to download a copy.
  2. Click the orange button, Get Form.
  3. Using your ordered pairs, use the circle to mark the locations of each point made by your patterns. You may need to make the circle smaller.
  4. Label the ordered pairs using the text tool.
  5. Click “Done” on the top right when you are finished filling in your coordinate plane.

Explain Your Pattern

Now that you have graphed your pattern on a coordinate plane, see if you can explain what you see. For example, when observing the graph and values from the example above, you can see that Pattern 2, which is the y-coordinate, increases at a faster rate than Pattern 1, the x-coordinate. In fact, the terms in Pattern 2 are three times the value of the corresponding terms in Pattern 1. Looking at the coordinate (1,3), 3 is three times 1. In coordinate (2,6), 6 is three times 2. If you keep going you will see that this relationship continues to be true for each coordinate pair. What is the relationship between the values in your coordinate pairs?
