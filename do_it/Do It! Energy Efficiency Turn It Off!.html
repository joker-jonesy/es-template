Do It! Vernier Elementary Energy Efficiency: Energy Saver or Energy Consumer

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to figure out ways to save energy, and to convince yourself and others to do it! First, figure out if there are ways that you can conserve energy. Are you an energy saver or can you improve your habits?

Compare your energy usage with your partner and together, come up with a plan for how each of you could save more energy on a daily basis. Use this information to help you build an argument for conserving energy. You will also calculate the average use of energy in your state—daily, weekly, monthly, and yearly—compare this usage to other states, and use this data to support your argument. You will include all of this important information on a poster, brochure, flyer, or in a presentation.

 Project Steps

  1. How Can You Become an Energy Saver?

  2. How Much Energy Do Your Appliances Use?

  3. How Does Your State Compare in Energy Usage?

  4. Share Your Message

How Can You Become an Energy Saver?

Everyday we make decisions that lead to energy conservation or energy use. Should we go to school using a bike, bus, or car? Do we turn the TV off when we aren’t watching it, or do we keep it on as background noise? Consider what you do during the average day. Think about how you spend your time and what electronic devices and appliances you are using. To get an idea of your energy usage, take this Energy Saver Survey. You will submit this survey as part of your project submission. 

Once you have taken the survey, reflect on your responses. Be proud of the ways in which you conserve energy, and consider ways in which you can improve your energy consumption. Start to think about how your answers and realizations could help convince others to save energy. You will include these ideas in your poster, flyer, or presentation. 

Reflection Questions:

  1. What did you learn from the Energy Saver Survey?
  2. Do you use a lot of energy or a little?
  3. What do you do that uses less energy?
  4. What do you do that uses more energy than is necessary?
  5. What changes can you reasonably make to use less energy?

Choose 1-3 areas in which you can improve your energy usage. Write these down and commit to change. Can you start shutting off lights and devices when you are done using them? Can you air dry or towel dry your washed dishes instead of using the dishwasher? Can you combine your laundry with other family members so that you are always washing full loads? 

How Much Energy Do Your Appliances Use?

All electronic appliances use energy, but some use more than others. Believe it or not, a toaster uses way more energy than a refrigerator: it takes 1200 watts to operate a toaster whereas a freezer uses 180 watts! However, a fridge is on 24 hours a day whereas you may use your toaster for 10 minutes. Rather than trying to save energy by toasting fewer bagels, you can make other small changes that have a big impact, like slightly turning up the temperature of your fridge so it does not have to use quite as much energy to keep itself cold 24 hours a day. 

To get a better idea of your own energy usage, use this Energy Use Calculator.

How Does Your State Compare in Energy Usage?

You should now have an idea of whether you are an energy saver, an energy consumer, or whether you are energy efficient. You should also recognize how much energy some of your appliances use on a daily basis. Now it is time to understand how you and other families in your state compare to average households in other states.  

  1. Look for your state’s average monthly energy usage in this article, Article: How Much Electricity on Average Do Homes in Your State Use? 

  2. Record this value and then use it to figure out the daily, weekly, and yearly energy use for the average household in your state. Round these values to the nearest thousandth.

  3. Consider where your state lies in the list of states. Is it one of the higher energy-use states? Is it one of the states that averages lower on the list? What do you think contributes to its comparatively high, low, or average energy use? 

  4. Choose another state that interests you. 

  5. Write down the average monthly energy use for this state.

  6. Calculate the daily, weekly, and yearly energy usage for the chosen state and round to the nearest thousandth.  

  7. Use comparison symbols >, =, and < to compare your state’s daily, weekly, monthly, and yearly usage to the average usage of the other state. 

  8. Reflect on the reasons why your state’s energy usage is less than, greater than, or equal to the usage of the other state. Write down your theories and reasoning. 

  9. Select another state and follow steps 5-8.

Share Your Message

Determine how you want to share your SAVE ENERGY message. How can you convince your classmates to use less energy or to change the way they use energy? Will you take a picture or share facts? Do you want to make people laugh or stop and think?

Use Microsoft Word, Powerpoint, Publisher, Adobe InDesign, or another program in your lab to make a poster, brochure, flyer, or presentation to share your important message. Encourage your classmates to conserve energy and help take care of our planet.
