Do It! Stellarium: Stars in the Distance

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to explore constellations and their stars using Stellarium. Then, use place value to describe and compare distances between stars and Earth. Create a presentation to share your findings.

 Project Steps

  1. Stellarium Tour

  2. Explore Constellations
  3. Measure Star Distance
  4. Create a Presentation

Explore Constellations

Explore the different constellations in the Stellarium sky.

  1. Turn off Atmosphere and Ground using the buttons in the bottom menu.
  2. Turn on Deep-sky objects.
  3. Turn on Constellation labels.
  4. Turn on Constellation lines and optional Constellation art.
  5. Go to the Starlore tab in the Sky and Viewing Options window found in the left menu. Check the "Show boundaries" box.

Which constellations interest you the most? Choose your favorite constellation to explore in more detail. 

Look around your constellation and look for unique or interesting objects.

  • Which stars are part of the constellation?
    NOTE: Some stars have nicknames shown next to each star. If not, other names also show up in the top left corner of the screen when the object is selected. If there is no nickname, use the name starting with "HIP" when you talk or write about it.
  • What other bright stars, nebulae, galaxies, or other cool objects can you find within its boundary lines?
  • Take screenshots of your constellation and some of your objects.
    • Windows Computer: Ctrl + S
    • Mac Computer: Command + S
    • The screenshots will automatically save to your desktop unless you've changed that setting in Stellarium. You can check by going to Tools in the Configuration window.
  • Go online and look up some interesting facts about your favorite constellation.
    • What is the constellation's history?
    • Are there any stories or myths written about it?

Measure Star Distance

  1. Research the distance (in light years) between Earth and stars within your chosen constellation. On the following website, click on the dropdown “Constellation List” and select your constellation. https://www.constellation-guide.com/constellation-list/andromeda-constellation/. 

  2. Scroll down to the sections of text related to each star; they should all note how many light years away the star is from Earth. Choose one star.

  3. Using the place value chart, record the distance between the star and Earth.

  • Example: The star Alpheratz in the Andromeda constellation is 97 light years from Earth. On your place value chart, you would record it at 9 tens and 7 ones.

  1. Choose another star whose distance from Earth contains a similar digit to your first star. Record the distance on your place value chart.

  • Example: The star NGC 7686 is 900 light years from Earth. On your place value chart, you would record it as 9 hundreds.

  1. Look at the two numbers. Compare each place value. What digit(s) do the two numbers share? What place values are they in? How many times greater is the digit in one place's value versus the other? See the example below. 

  • Example:  We are comparing the star Alpheratz, which is 97 light years from Earth to the star NGC 7686, which is 900. Both numbers contain the digit 9. The 9 in 900 is 10 times larger than the 9 in 97 since 100 x 9 = 900 and 10 x 9 = 90.

Create a Presentation

Organize the information about your constellation and its objects. Create a presentation summarizing what you learned in Google Slides.

Open Google Slides. Click File, then New, then Presentation.

Create a slideshow with pictures of your constellation, facts that you researched, and the place value chart, which should include the two star distances.
