Do It! Vernier Elementary Energy Efficiency: Keep Our City Cool

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to use the infrared thermometer to measure the heat generated by different materials. Then, consider how the materials we use to build our city impact the temperature of our cities. Create a pie chart to represent the surface materials that make up a certain area by measuring the size of each slice on the pie chart and drawing it on a circle. Finally, make recommendations to help minimize your contribution to urban heat islands.

Choose an area from your everyday life that would be found in a city or the metropolitan area surrounding it, such as your school, a shopping center, or a house. You will focus your data collection and pie chart fractions on the surface materials found in the area you choose. Write down the name or a description of this area.

Use the infrared thermometer to measure the temperature of various city materials including concrete, metal, windows, asphalt, planters, and street signs. Be sure to include materials that are found in the area you chose. Plan to measure the temperatures of at least five different materials.

Start by taking the temperature of the surrounding air so you can compare this temperature to the materials you are measuring. You should also note the time of day and weather conditions since your results will be affected by these conditions.

Using the Infrared Camera

The infrared thermometer can be used portably to collect individual material temperatures, or with the computer and the Go!Link cord to create a graph of the temperature readings with the Logger Lite software.

  1. Turn on the infrared thermometer (IR thermometer) by pushing the “MEAS” button (this is required for portable use and if you are using the computer to collect and review data).
  2. Point the sensor at the material you are measuring for temperature (the IR thermometer works only in degrees Celsius, but it will convert the temperature for you if you use the Logger Lite software and select the “Switch C/F” button).
  3. The temperature measurement is acquired from a circular region of the material. The laser button on the IR thermometer helps you determine the spot from which your sensor is collecting temperature data. The closer you are to the object you are measuring, the more accurate your reading. The farther away it is, the bigger the data spot. Use caution when using the tool.
  4. The “Hold” button will freeze the temperature measurement when you are ready to write down your data. To unfreeze your temperature reading, press the “Hold” button again.
  5. To turn the sensor off, push the “MEAS” button again. The sensor will automatically shut off in 30 minutes.

Preparing Your Pie Chart

You will create a pie chart to represent the types of surface materials that currently make up the area you chose. Each piece of the pie chart should represent a different material and the fraction of space it occupies  in the chosen area . If the area has more of a certain type of surface material, that piece of the pie chart will be larger. Your pie chart should represent all of the major surface materials in the area you chose, so your fractions need to add up to one whole.

Grass Dirt Concrete Asphalt
¼ ½ ⅛ ⅛
¼ + ½ + ⅛ + ⅛ = 1

Even though you can probably estimate the size for some of the pieces in your pie chart, to make it most accurate, use a protractor to draw the exact size of each piece.

Strat by changing your fractions into degrees. To do this, multiply each fraction by 360. This will tell you the number of degrees in each piece of your pie chart. For example, let’s say that one quarter of the area you chose is an asphalt parking lot. To figure out the area of the pie chart that needs to represent the asphalt, multiply one quarter by 360:

1/4 × 360/1 = 360/4 = 90

Record the fractions and degrees calculated for each surface material. You will need this information to create the pie chart.

Creating Your Pie Chart

Create a pie chart using Google Slides or a similar presentation software. If you use  Google Slides, you can follow these instructions:

1. Use the shape tool to draw a circle.

Hold down Shift while expanding the circle to make sure it stays a circle and does not stretch into an oval.

Use the guides to mark the center of the circle. Draw a horizontal line from the center point to the outer edge of the circle.

You may want to make the line a bright color or increase the line weight in order to see it more clearly. To do so, make sure the line is selected. Then, go to the top menu bar and choose “Format”, then “Borders & lines”, and finally “Line color” or “Line weight”.

2. Copy and paste this image of the protractor onto your slide. Resize it if needed.

3.  Line up the protractor so that the existing line forms one ray of your angle.

Put the endpoint of the angle in the center point of the protractor. One ray should always go through the 0° point on the protractor.

4. Find the number of degrees you calculated for one of the surface materials and make a mark in the appropriate place on the protractor.

The place where the other ray crosses is the measure of your angle.

5. Draw a new line from the center of your circle to your mark. Label this pie piece with the correct number of degrees, the fraction, and the surface material it represents.

In the example below, this piece represents 72° of the 360° in the whole circle. How can you think of this piece as a fraction of the whole circle?

Decreasing Heat Islands

Look over the data you gathered using the infrared thermometer and think about how each type of surface affects the area.

  • Which surfaces have the highest surface temperatures?
  • Which surfaces have the lowest surface temperatures?
  • What changes could be made to the area so that the surfaces contribute less to urban heat islands?
  • Are there types of surfaces you would add or remove completely? What could replace them?
  • Are there surfaces you would increase or decrease in size?

Use the previous steps to draw a new pie chart to reflect the changes you would make. Remember: since your area represents one whole, you will need to adjust the size of each piece so that all of your fractions still add up to 1.
