Do It! Snap Circuits Electricity: Build Series and Parallel Circuits

 The Challenge

What are different ways to create circuits with more than one light or power source? Your challenge is to learn about parallel and series circuits as you build and test different types of circuit designs. Then, build circuits to fit specific measures of perimeter and area, and learn how to convert unit measurements for the circuits you build.

 Getting Started

Beware of Short Circuits

Remember to Avoid Short Circuits! Any electrical path without resistance from a load like a light or motor is a short circuit. Short circuits will DAMAGE your Snap Circuits kit. Be sure to check all possible electrical paths for short circuits BEFORE connecting your circuit to a power source. Review short circuits in the Information about Snap Circuits Electricity section.

Build a Series Circuit

Build the circuit shown below. Notice that it uses two lights. Note: Your kit may have two L1 lights OR one L1 and one L2 light.

When two lights are connected in a line one behind the other, it is called a series circuit.

This is like having two cars driving single file in the same lane.

Test your series circuit and note the brightness of the bulbs. How does it compare to the circuit with one light?

Review the What You Should Know: How Does a Light Bulb Work? section. Find out how electricity runs through a light bulb. The light bulb is actually part of the wiring path. What do you think happens when the filament breaks or we unscrew the bulb?

While we cannot actually unscrew the bulb in our Snap Circuits kit, we can create the same effect by disconnecting the wiring path right next to one of the bulbs. Observe what happens. Do one or both of the lights go off?

Build a Parallel Circuit

Now, build another circuit with two lights, but connect them side-by-side instead of in a line.

Build the circuit shown below. Note: your kit may have two L1 lights OR one L1 and one L2 light.

When lights are connected side-by-side(so that they each have their own path) it is called a parallel circuit.

This is like having two cars driving side-by-side in their own lanes.

Test your circuit and observe what happens. How does the brightness of the bulbs compare to the series circuit?

Repeat the same experiment you did with the series circuit to see what happens when a bulb is unscrewed or the bulb burns out (i.e. the filament breaks). Disconnect one of the wiring connectors as shown.

Test your circuit and observe what happens: Do one or both lights go out? How does the parallel circuit compare to the series circuit?

When a bulb burns out or is unscrewed in a series circuit, the wiring path is broken for the whole circuit. Since there is only one possible path for the electricity, it cannot flow to any bulb in the circuit. None of the bulbs will work.

When a bulb burns out or is unscrewed in a parallel circuit, the wiring path is only interrupted for that part of the circuit.

There is still a possible path for the electricity to flow to other bulbs in the circuit. Only one bulb is affected, so other bulbs will work.

Did you also notice that the bulbs were brighter in the parallel circuit than in the series circuit?

In a series circuit, the voltage from the power supply is shared by all the bulbs in the circuit.

Voltage is one measure of electrical energy.

When two identical bulbs are in a series circuit, they each only get half as much voltage and cannot glow as brightly.

In a parallel circuit, the voltage from the power supply is not shared by the bulbs in the circuit.

Each bulb in a parallel circuit gets the same amount of voltage as it would if it were the only bulb in the circuit, so they glow just as brightly.

When does it make sense to use a parallel circuit instead of a series circuit? What kind of circuit do you think is used most often in your house?
