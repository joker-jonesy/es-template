Do It! Google Slides: Describe a Mystery Object

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to create a Google Slide presentation that describes a mystery object of your choosing. You will give clues using your five senses to help people try to determine what the mystery object is before the big reveal.

 Getting Started

  1. Log in to your Google Account and go to your Google Drive.
  2. On the left side of the screen at the top, click + NEW.
  3. On the drop down menu, click Google Slides.

 Project Steps

  1. Plan your Layout

  2. Create your Presentation

  3. Present your Slides
  4. Vote and Gather Data
  5. The Big Reveal

  6. Using your Data

Plan your Layout

Start by planning out the order that you want your slides to go in. Remember to include the information you brainstormed in the Plan and SMART Goal assignment. 
You need a slide for each sense: hearing, sight, touch, smell, and taste. 

Create your Presentation

Using Google Slides, create your presentation.

Include:

  1. Cover slide with your name
  2. Slides 2-6: descriptive words, close-up images, audio, close-up video, senses affected by your object
  3. Slide 7: blank or voting slide
  4. Slide 8: the big reveal - Show your mystery object!! Make it jump off the page with how you design the background.
  5. Review and edit your slides

Present your Slides

Gather your audience and present slides 1-6.

Remember to go back to the tips on how to keep your audience engaged in the What You Should Know section.

Vote and Gather Data

  1. Ask your audience if they think they know what your mystery object is.
  2. Choose your voting method (public or anonymous).
  3. Conduct your voting to gather your data. Make sure all voices are heard and all choices are marked down.

The Big Reveal

Show your audience slide 8. Talk to your audience about how many people were right.

Using your Data

After presenting, take all of the data you have collected and think about the best way to display the information. 

  1. Depending on which voting method you choose, organize your data.
  2. Ask yourself the questions noted in Using Data to Make Graphs in the background section.
    • Who am I presenting this information to?
    • What information am I presenting?
    • How much data do I have?
  1. Create the graph/chart that best fits the data you collected.
  2. If made on the computer, save the document; if created on paper, set aside to turn in.
  3. Bonus: Compare some of the voting choices and make them into fractional statements. For example, using the milkshake example from above, 3/10 of the people asked liked banana milkshakes best.

What does the data tell you about your presentation? Did you give enough detail? Did you give too much detail?

Revise your presentation if you want to make it a little easier or harder for your audience to guess.
