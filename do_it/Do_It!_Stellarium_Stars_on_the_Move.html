Do It! Stellarium: Tracking Stars

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to use Stellarium to speed up time in order to see how different objects move across the sky. Use what you learn to draw your own model of our solar system and its motions. Then, collect data on the hours of visibility for the Sun, Moon, and planets, and use the data to create a line plot.

Time Controls Following Objects

The universe is full of motion, but much of it is too slow to see in real time. Stellarium can rewind, pause, and fast-forward time to speed up objects in the sky. Navigate to the bottom menu to find these buttons. Click the fast-forward and rewind buttons more times to speed them up even more. The down arrow button returns you to the current time and date.

When you speed up Stellarium using the Center button found in the bottom menu, you might find that the screen gets "wobbly" when you follow a specific object. To fix this, turn on the Equatorial mount button, which is also found in the bottom menu. The actual button says, “Switch between equatorial and azimuthal mount” when you hover over it.

This makes your view follow in the same direction as the celestial sphere's rotation, so it will smoothly move with the sky.

Celestial Speed-Up

Use Stellarium to observe and explore how the many objects in the sky travel around the sky. 

Celestial Sphere Motion

Get started by seeing how the entire celestial sphere appears to move over time.

  1. Make sure the Center and the Equatorial mount buttons are OFF to begin. Turn ON the Ground.
  2. Start to fast-forward using the Time Controls. What direction does the celestial sphere move? Bonus: What does this tell you about the actual direction the Earth is spinning?
  3. Now turn ON the Equatorial mount button. You are now moving with the celestial sphere, making the ground look like it is spinning instead.
  4. Next, turn the Ground OFF and play with the speed some more. Remember: your screen is still moving with the celestial sphere, so the stars should not be moving across the screen.
  5. You might see other little dots moving in random directions very quickly between the stars as you fast forward. What are these?
  6. Continue to experiment and get comfortable with the time controls and object motions.

Try slowing down the fast-forward just enough so that you can click on one of these fast-moving objects. What "type" of object does Stellarium say it is?

These objects are artificial satellites—man-made objects sent into orbit around the Earth. This includes things like satellites, space telescopes, the International Space Station, and "space junk" from old satellites or rocket parts.

Solar System Tracking

Now take a closer look at the motions of our solar system's objects.

  1. Keep the Equatorial mount button turned ON and Center your view on the Moon or a planet to follow it through the sky.
  2. Go to the SSO tab of the Sky and viewing options window found in the left menu and turn on "Show planet markers" and "show orbits." Deselect “Only orbit for selected object.” This will show the path of all the planets and their moons.

  3. Turn Equatorial mount OFF. Adjust the time speed and watch the object's motion. How does it compare to the background stars?
  4. Explore the motions of other objects. Can you see the many moons of Jupiter or Saturn orbiting their planets? (You may need to Zoom in using CTRL+ Up Arrow or the roller on your mouse.)
  5. Go to the Location window and change your "Planet" to Solar System Observer. You are now seeing the entire solar system from an outside location in space!
  6. If you want a less messy view, go back to the SSO tab of the Sky and viewing options window and check the "Only orbits of major planets" box.
  7. Take a look around and see how each planet and their moons orbit the Sun.

Solar System Sketch

You have watched the motions of our solar system from different views and speeds. Now use what you learned to recreate our solar system on paper or with a computer program of your choice. Go back to Stellarium as needed.

  • Include the Sun, Moon, and planets.
  • Draw each object's orbit path.
  • Use arrows to show which direction each object is moving around the Sun/planet compared to other objects.
  • Remember to label everything in your picture.

Collect Hours of Visibility Data

Celestial objects are only visible at certain times of the day, which depend on the viewer’s location. Astronomers who study these celestial objects need to know when they will be visible in order to plan their research time. You might also want to know when they are visible so you can look for them in the sky. Collect information about the visibility of celestial objects in your location and in one other.

  1. In Stellarium, select the Astronomical Calculations Window in the left menu.
  2. Click on WUT (What’s Up Tonight).
  3. Under Select a Category choose Planets.  The default setting lists the planets that are potentially visible from your location this evening. You can use the dropdown to change the selection to planets visible at midnight, in the morning, or at any time of the night. Next to the planet name is information, including the rise and set times.
  4. Use the rise and set information to determine the hours of potential visibility for three celestial objects. If there aren’t three in your list, change the timing from In the Evening to In Any Time of the Night.
  5. For each of the three celestial objects, you will need to calculate the number of hours from the rise time on the first day to midnight, and add this value to the number of hours from midnight to the time the object sets the next day.
    For example, suppose Mars has the following rise/set information:
    Rise: 15:57; Set: 2:18
    To determine the hours of visibility, you would calculate the number of hours from 15:57 to midnight that night (or 24:00 since there are 24 hours in a day). Then you would calculate the number of hours from midnight the next morning (or 0:00 since we reset our counter to 0 when we start counting a new day) to 2:18. Finally, we add these two values together, as shown in the table.
  6. Round each data value to the nearest ½ hour. In the example above, 10 hours and 21 minutes would round to 10 hours.
  7. Choose a different location. Repeat the process to collect the hours of visibility for celestial objects at that location using Stellarium.

Create Hours of Visibility Line Plot

Scientists often use dot plots to analyze scientific data. Dot plots can help organize information and visualize data better than having lists of data. Use the data you collected about hours of visibility of celestial objects to create line plots.

  1. Create two line plots with the data you collected, one for each location. Use the same number line for each line plot.
  2. Answer the following questions using your line plots.
    1. How did you determine what the minimum and maximum values on the number line should be?
    2. How similar are the line plots?
    3. Which location had the celestial object with the greatest number of hours of visibility? Which object was it?
    4. How many more hours is the  object visible in this location when compared to the other location?
    5. Which location had the celestial object with the least number of hours of visibility? Which object is it?
    6. How many fewer hours is the object visible in this location when compared to the other location?
    7. For each location, what is the average number of hours of visibility for the celestial objects? To find the average, add up all the data values and divide by the number of values.
