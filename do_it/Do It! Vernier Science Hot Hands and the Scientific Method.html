Do It! Vernier Science: Scientific Method

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to learn how to do an experiment using the scientific method. You will practice the scientific method by doing a class temperature experiment using the Vernier Go!Temp sensors and the Graphical Analysis software to see who has the hottest hand. Then, redo the experiment using place value relationships to set new time and sample intervals.

Class Experiment

Select each item to learn more.

Step #1: Ask a Question

In this experiment, you will measure the hand temperature of everyone in the class.

What are some questions you can ask about hand temperature?

Here are some ideas to get you started:

  • In our class, do boys or girls have warmer hands?
  • What is the average hand temperature of the class?
  • Do taller classmates have warmer hands?
  • How big of a difference is there between the warmest hand and the coldest hand in the class?

Talk about other possible questions with your class and decide which one you would all like to explore first.

Step #2: State a Hypothesis

Make a prediction. What do you think your experiment will tell you about your question?

Here are some possible hypotheses for the sample questions in Step #1:

  • We think girls have warmer hands than boys.
  • We think the average hand temperature of the class is 98 degrees Fahrenheit.
  • We do not think a person's height makes a difference in hand temperature.
  • We think everyone’s hand temperature will be within five degrees Fahrenheit.

Write down your own hypothesis for your class question. Each person can have his or her own hypothesis. Just remember: It is not important if your prediction is incorrect. It simply provides a purpose for your experiment. It is the statement to prove or disprove.

Test your hypothesis by collecting data through an experiment.

Start by watching the following video on how to use your sensor with the Graphical Analysis software. Feel free to come back to this video if you get stuck later.

When you finish the tutorial:

  1. As a class, decide if you will measure in degrees Fahrenheit or degrees Celsius.
  2. Connect the Go!Temp sensor to your computer.
  3. Launch the Graphical Analysis software.
  4. Select Sensor Data Collection.
  5. Connect your sensor.

Hold the temperature sensor tightly in your hand and click the COLLECT data button. The computer will record your hand temperature for 60 seconds.

Look at your data table. How hot were your hands? Find out by looking at the last temperature collected at the bottom of your data table.

Click on the three dots next to Data Set 1. Click on Rename Data Set. Type in your name. When you click on COLLECT again, it will create another Data Set for the next person in your group.

Repeat this process for every member at your station.

Step #4: Analyze Your Data

Share your experimental data with your class as instructed by your facilitator. Make a graph or chart that shows the combined class information in a useful way.

What do you observe from the data your class collected? Do you notice any patterns or trends? How can you use this data to answer the question for your experiment?

Step #5: Draw a Conclusion

Based on your experiment results, what seems to be the answer to your question? Did the findings support your hypothesis?

Be sure to support your statement by including information from Step #4. Think about how you could improve your class experiment. Are all of the temperature readings accurate? What might have influenced some students' hand temperatures, but not others?

Adjust the Intervals

Now that you’ve had practice using the temperature sensors and the scientific method, use what you’ve learned to perform a second experiment. Remember that the default for the Go!Temp probe is 50 samples/second, which means that the data interval is 0.02 seconds.

  • If you wanted to reduce the number of samples taken to 0.5 samples/second, what would the new interval be? If you wanted to increase the number of samples taken to 500 samples/second, what would the new interval be?
    • (500 samples/second might seem like a lot, but consider audio recordings in a music studio: they take samples of analog signals—used to record music—at a rate of 41,100 times per second!)

In this second experiment, collect the same data as in the class experiment, but this time change the rate for the number of samples taken each second.

  • What do you predict the new graph will look like?
  • How will it be different from the graph generated using the default of 50 samples/second?

Change the Data Interval

  1. To change the default interval, click on the Data Collections button on the lower left of the computer screen. The button starts with the word Mode.
  2. Click on the ‘50’ displayed in the ‘Rate’ box and enter the new rate that you will use when collecting data for this new experiment; you can choose to either increase or decrease the number of samples taken each second.
  3. After setting the new rate, select Done and test it out.

When using the scientific method while conducting an experiment, it’s good practice to record your process. The Scientific Method data sheet is one example of a science or lab report form that could be used, and it’s the sheet you will use to record your experiment.

Select each item to learn more.

Step #1: Ask a Question

Begin by filling in the question: “When comparing the graph of data measuring the temperature of hands at a rate of 50 samples taken/second to the graph of data collected at a rate of ____ samples taken/second, how will the graphs differ?”

Fill in the blanks with the information specific to your experiment and record in the ‘Question:’ box on the sheet.

Step #2: State a Hypothesis

What do you predict will happen to the graph? What is your hypothesis? Record this in the ‘Hypothesis:” box on the sheet.

Step #3: Do an Experiment

Now it’s time to do the experiment. The procedure for this experiment must be exactly the same as the procedure you followed when collecting data for the class experiment. Record your materials and procedures in the correct boxes; you can write out the procedure as a numbered list:

  1. Change the rate in the software to _____ samples/second.
  2. Make a list of the names of students who will be tested.
  3. …..

Continue in this way until you have all of the steps listed. Be specific; someone picking up your sheet should be able to recreate your experiment in exactly the same way. Once the boxes are filled in, begin collecting data.

Step #4: Analyze Your Data

When finished collecting data, create a graph that displays the collected data in the same way you completed the graph for the class experiment.  What do you notice? Record your observations on the sheet in the ‘Observations/Data:’ box and take a screenshot of the graph you created.

Step #5: Draw a Conclusion

Now it’s time to answer your question. Did the data support your question? Record the answer to your question in the ‘Conclusion:’ box.


Complete ‘The Scientific Method’ sheet to share your findings with the class, turning in both the sheet and any graphs created.
