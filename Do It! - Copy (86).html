Taking Care of Snap Circuits

Each Snap Circuits piece has electrical components to make it work in your circuit. These pieces can be damaged if they're not taken care of properly. Use the tips below to prevent your pieces from being broken.

Attaching Pieces to the Grid

  1. Set the Snap Circuits piece on the grid and line up the snaps with the pegs on the grid, or the snaps of another piece.
  2. Put a finger on each of the snaps on the top piece and push evenly.

Removing Pieces from the Grid

  1. Place two fingers on each side of the Snap Circuits piece.
  2. Carefully lift the piece directly up.
  3. If the piece is too hard to remove this way, tilt it sideways to remove. When removing the battery holder, tilt the holder towards the snaps to remove.

    

Beware of Short Circuits

Warning

Follow this IMPORTANT RULE so that you don't damage the parts or hurt yourself: ALL CIRCUITS NEED A LOAD.

The load (like a light bulb or motor) creates resistance in the circuit. The resistance uses and slows down the electric current.

When a circuit does not have enough resistance, it is called a short circuit. In a short circuit, the electricity flows too fast. If this happens, it can damage electrical parts and drain batteries. With enough power, the parts can get hot enough to start a fire. Fortunately, Snap Circuits kits do not have enough power for that.

Before connecting power, always check to be sure your circuit does not have a short.

Reflect

When you flipped the motor, it changed the way the current flowed through it. 

Don't forget about the current direction when you build your Snap Circuits projects. If a circuit does not work correctly, check to see if a part is backward.

Project #13 – Two-Speed Fan

What do you think will happen if you build a circuit with both a light and a motor? Both loads use power from the battery. 

Build the two-speed fan and make a prediction about what will happen when you push down on the press switch. Keep in mind that electricity will take the easiest path in a circuit. Any load, like a light, makes it harder for electricity to flow through it by creating resistance. 

A simple wire is much easier to move through. 

Notice where the spacer is used when building your circuit. 

Now test your prediction.

  • Turn the slide switch to the "on" position. Observe the light and the fan. Are they both turned on? How fast is the fan spinning?
  • Now press and hold the press switch. What changed? Are both the light and motor still on? How fast is the fan spinning?

Was your prediction correct? Explain in your own words why pressing the press switch had the effect it did. Take a picture of your circuit. Use arrows to label the current path both when the press switch is "off" and when it is "on."
