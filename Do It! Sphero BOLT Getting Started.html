Do It! Sphero: Germs Everywhere

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to program Sphero BOLT to simulate the spread of an infectious disease. Learn how to program the BOLT robot to communicate with other BOLT robots to “spread a disease”, and use a line plot to track the spread of the disease. You will conduct at least three trials so you can find the average number of robots infected in the specific time intervals. You will also do calculations to find the total number of infected robots at each time interval.

 Getting Started

You will use the Sphero Edu app to control your robot. If you do not already have one, create an account on Sphero Edu.

Getting started is simple.

  1. Make sure your Sphero BOLT is charged. You will see a solid white light on the charger when it is fully charged.
  2. Log into the Sphero Edu app on your device.
  3. Tap the "Connect Robot" button and select your robot to connect to your Sphero BOLT. (Your device ID is written just below the little white Sphero logo on the internal hardware of the Sphero BOLT.)
  4. Select the Activities icon on the bottom of your device screen.
  5. Select the Sphero tab at the top of the screen.

Epidemiologists study an infection, the spread of infection, and the cure. You are the epidemiologist in this project; you will program a contagious infection in order to study its behavior.

Remember the following differences between this simulation model and the real world of infectious diseases:

  1. Not all people who “pick up” a germ actually become sick.
  2. A person with the infection will only infect others for a short time, usually about a week. The common cold is considered infectious when you are coughing or sneezing, which is about 2 weeks.
  3. Once a person has recovered from the disease, they are no longer contagious.
  4. The actual number of infected people can be more or less than the model predicts.

Program the First Infected Robot

  1. Draw a simple line plot. The x-axis should show time, such as 30-second intervals. You will stack X’s vertically to represent each robot during that time interval.
    1. You probably want to wait on deciding the time interval until after you have tested out your programs. You don’t want to choose 30-second intervals if all of your robots get infected in the first 10 seconds, and the spread depends in part on the settings you choose for your programs. 
  2. Open a new workspace under the Programs tab and press the “+”sign.
  3. Name the program "Infection" or something similar so you can identify your program later.
  4. Select the program type that uses blocks and select create.
  5. Navigate to the controls tab and add a loop forever block to the on start program block. Adding this block will allow the virus to spread repeatedly through sending Infrared (IR) messages.
  6. Navigate to the lights tab and add a matrix animation block within the loop. Choose the image or animation of your choice to represent the virus.

Just as infected people can go all over and meet random people, so can your robot.  Program the infected robot to roll continuously in a random pattern to spread the disease. Try the following steps to add a rolling block to the program:

  1. Navigate to the movements tab and add a roll block after the matrix animation block inside of the loop forever block.
  2. Select the operators tab and locate the random int block.
  3. Add a random int block into the heading, speed, and duration circles in the roll block.
  4. Experiment with each setting to decide the direction, speed, and duration of your roll. Hit the Start button on your program to watch how your choices affect the movements of your Sphero. If you are not happy with the way Sphero moves, hit Stop, adjust the settings, and try again.
    • Heading - In which direction do you want the infected BOLT to move?
    • Speed - How fast should BOLT move through the community?
    • Duration - How long should each movement last?

Program the Spread

Now, it is time to spread the infection to other robots. Add the following blocks to your existing code to enable BOLT to communicate with the other robots and spread the infection.

  1. Locate and tap the Communications tab. Add a send message block below the roll block. Be sure to keep the message channel at 4.
  2. Set the IR intensity between 1-64. The lower the number, the closer other robots will need to be to receive the message.
    • If you have a smaller space to work with, you probably want to use a smaller number. If your robots could potentially roll around a large lab, you may want to choose a larger number. Once you program the healthy robots, you will want to do a few test runs to figure out the best IR intensity for your space.
    • Relate this to how a virus spreads, both by touching and by coughs or sneezes. (This distance depends on the disease. Some diseases are more communicable, or contagious, than others. In terms of your program, this is based on the IR intensity level you choose.)

Program the Healthy Robots

Healthy robots will receive the infection and spread it themselves. You need to program the other robots to receive the infection.

  1. On an iPad linked to another Sphero, open a new block program canvas and name it “Healthy.”
  2. Add a matrix animation block to the on start program block. Create or select an animation for all of the healthy robots to display before they are infected.
  3. Select the events tab, and add an ‘on message’ block. Be sure that the channel is set to.
  4. Drag a new matrix animation block below the on message block. Select an animation that shows the infection transmitting.
  5. Aside from the change in animation, you can also represent the infection transmission with movement and sound. Here is one idea, but you can come up with your own movements and sounds:
    • Place a for loop block below the matrix block and set it to repeat 2 times.
    • You can add two spin blocks and set them to make BOLT twitch and move as it becomes infected.
    • You can also add a play sound block after the loop. Under personality, you can find the vomit sound. This way your robot will sound like it is vomiting after it becomes infected.
  1. Recreate the program from Part 1: Program the First Infected Robot. Add the loop forever block and others directly after the play sound block.
  2. Repeat these steps for each Sphero.

You can confine the BOLTS to a smaller area by putting barriers such as boxes or chairs.

  1. Test your programs to see if the infection transmits, and how far apart the robots are when they do transmit.
  2. Use a timer to figure out how long it could take for the disease to spread to at least one other robot.
  3. Revise the IR intensity and other settings as needed in order for the robots to transmit the infection in a reasonable period of time. (For example, if you have 6 robots, a reasonable amount of time for all 6 to get infected may be 1-5 minutes).
  4. Choose a time interval based on how long it took for one robot to get the disease. (See the paragraphs above for more guidance and explanation).

  1. To warm up, calculate the total number of robots infected at each time interval. Do this calculation for each simulation trial.
