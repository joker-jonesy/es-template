Your challenge is to come up with a plan to excite your schoolmates to learn about global climate change. Play the games and review the activities on NASA's Climate Kids website to find ones that would interest your peers while teaching them something valuable about climate change. 

If you have extra time, design your own game or activity to teach about global climate change, clean energy, or another topic found on NASA's Climate Kids website. You don't have to program a working game; you can simply sketch it out using a tool like drawing software.

Find a Fun Resource for Your Peers

Choosing Your Resource

You do not have to choose a game or activity; you can choose a video, interview, image gallery, article, or even a single photo. However, if you choose one of these, you will want to come up with an interesting way of presenting it.

For example, if you choose a video, you may ask your peers to guess what it's about based on the video title and thumbnail. Then, stop the video in the middle and ask your peers to guess what happens next. You can even ask your peers to come up with a different ending.

Step

Browse through NASA's Climate Kids website. Review games, activities, interviews, videos, image galleries, or any other resource that you think would interest your peers.

  1. Take notes in your ePortfolio. List the resources you review and briefly describe whether they would interest your peers and why. 
  2. After reviewing at least 3-5 different resources, choose one or two to focus on.
    • In your ePortfolio, explain why the chosen resource(s) would be the best to get your peers excited to learn. What is this resource? What is so interesting about it?
    • Explain the educational value of the resource. What would your peers learn from it?  List at least the facts and ideas someone would take away from it.
    • Is there a special way you would present the game, activity, or resource to make it even more interesting to your peers? If so, explain what you would do. 
  3. Review an article or two related to the topics covered by your game, activity, or resource. You can find a list of helpful articles under Climate Kids' Big Questions in the What You Should Know section, or you can do your own search on NASA's Climate Kids website.
    • List the article(s) you read and key takeaways in your ePortfolio.
    • After reading the articles, choose a few more key facts and ideas related to your game or resource. Once you have gotten your peers interested in the topic, you could share these facts and ideas with them. You could even suggest that they read the articles you chose.

Design Your Own Game or Activity

Design a game or activity that would excite your peers to learn about climate change or another topic from NASA's Climate Kids website. You can use drawing software to create a storyboard or game board. Or, you can draw something on paper and include a photo of it in your ePortfolio. You can also use existing pictures and graphics from the internet. You do not have to program a working game.
