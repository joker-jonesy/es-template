Important Vocabulary

  • Load - the device being powered in a complete circuit
  • Pattern - a repeating or recurring sequence
  • Power Source - where the energy comes from to power a device
  • Odd Number - a number that cannot be divided into equal groups
  • Even Number - a number that can be divided into equal groups

What Is Electricity?

Electricity powers many devices we use every day. Examples are lights, motors, cell phones, and computers. If it has a plug or a battery, it uses electricity. Look around the room. How many things can you find that run on electricity?

Everything in the universe is made of tiny particles called atoms. Atoms are too small to see—even with a normal microscope. How small are they? Try this: use a pencil to make a single dot on a piece of paper.

Can you guess how many atoms are in that dot?

Did you guess over 4,000,000,000,000,000,000 atoms? It is true. Pencil lead is made from graphite, which is a type of carbon, so your dot has over 4 billion billion carbon atoms.

Electricity is made up of moving electrons. Electrons move around the outside of the atom like the moon orbits around the Earth. Protons and neutrons cluster in the middle part of the atom. This is called the nucleus. In some materials, like in some electrical wires, electrons can easily separate from the rest of the atom.

Each electron has a small negative (-) electrical charge. Electrons are attracted to positive (+) charges, like protons. Think of the old saying, “opposites attract.” Electrons in a wire leave their atoms and are “pulled” towards the positive end of the wire.

Electrons can also “push” each other around. They do not want to be near one another. They want to be near their opposites (protons). Electrons are “pulled” to the positive end of the wire, but they also “push” each other in that same direction. They are trying to find the positive charge. This flow of electrons is called an electric current. This steady flow is the source of energy that is electricity.

Imagine a group of people trying to find seats in a movie theater. They walk through the aisle in search of empty seats. The people are like the electrons and the seats are like the protons. The people are drawn to the seats because they know that is where they belong. Plus, they feel the pressure from the people behind them to keep moving towards the seats so that everyone can find their place.

Voltage is like the “push” or the “pressure.” The pressure of the pushing on the electrons of a wire becomes the energy that powers our devices.

What Is a Circuit?

Electrons need to move around a continuous (unbroken) path to create electricity. This path is called a circuit. The wires and power cords for our devices are the paths for the electrons.

In a way, electricity is like the water that flows down a river and the circuit is the riverbed.

We can design these circuits in many different ways to control electricity for specific jobs. These jobs can be as simple as turning a light bulb on and off, or as complex as controlling the flight of a rocket.

Why Do Cords Have Two Prongs?

Now think about electrical power cords. Why do you think they have two prongs? (Prongs are the metal parts that stick out of the end of the cord).

Each prong is connected to a wire; one for the electrical current to flow from the wall outlet to the device (like a light) and another to carry the current back to the wall (completing the circuit and making it a continuous path). Plastic or rubber covers the wires to keep us safe from electrical shock. (Electricity does not travel well through these materials.)

All Circuits Work in the Same Way

All circuits work in the same basic way. Electricity flows from a power source (like a battery) along a wiring path to an electrical device (like a light), and then back.

We will use the word “load” for the electrical device. A load is anything that needs electricity to work.

All circuits have these three parts:

  1. Power source
  2. Load
  3. Wiring path

Look at the example circuit. Do you notice the arrows that show the direction of the electrical current? The direction is important because some electrical parts work differently—or not at all—depending on which way the current flows. The “+" and “-” sides of a power source, like the battery shown here, are called poles.

Although the electrons physically move from the negative (-) pole to the positive (+) pole, we usually show current arrows as going from the + to - pole. (Research “Benjamin Franklin and electric current” for the detailed history.)

How Does a Light Switch Work?

How do we turn a light on and off?

Remember that we need a continuous path for electrons to move and create electricity. If we want to control when the light goes on and off, all we have to do is add something to our circuit that can “break” and reconnect the circuit loop.

Almost all circuits also have some type of switch that can do just that.

When the switch is “on,” it connects the wires to make a continuous path. The current can move through the circuit to power the light.

When the switch is “off,” the circuit path is broken. The current stops flowing and the light turns off.

Identifying Number Patterns

A number pattern is a pattern or sequence in a series of numbers. Number patterns occur all the time in our everyday lives: For example, the number of petals on a specific kind of flower, or the number of eggs in a cartoon. These are predictable and can be mapped out.

The Snap Circuit kit has snap wire pieces that have 3 snaps or contact points. What pattern are you creating if you keep adding 3-snap wires to your circuit, one at a time? Your pattern will look like this for the first ten snaps:

What patterns do you see?

Patterns with Addition -The numbers count up by three each time. 3, 6, 9, 12… One pattern is repeated addition: 3 + 3 + 3 + 3…This means each piece is a multiple of three.

Patterns with Multiplication - You can multiply the number of pieces by three to find the total number of snaps. For example, the first piece (1) has 3 snaps: 1 × 3 = 3. By adding a second piece (2), there are 6 snaps: 2 × 3 = 6. By adding a third piece (3), there are 9 snaps: 3 × 3 = 9. You can show this in a table.

Career Connection and Real-World Application

Budget Analyst

If you like finding mathematical patterns, you may choose a career as a budget analyst or accountant. Every company, big and small, has a budget. This is the money a company brings in and spends. A budget analyst finds patterns in budgets and recommends changes so that companies have bigger profits. A profit is the money left over after all of the expenses have been paid. An accountant finds patterns in budget to help companies save money, pay their employees and bills, and calculate profit.

Climatologist

Another career that uses mathematical patterns is a climatologist. A climatologist studies climate conditions and general weather patterns. They can make predictions about future weather patterns and use patterns in data to protect animals and their habitats.

Electricians

Electricians install or update wiring in buildings. They conduct tests to improve the flow and fix problems with electricity. Electricians work outside on power lines or indoors at power plants.
