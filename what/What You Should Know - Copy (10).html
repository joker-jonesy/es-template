What You Should Know

Types of Circuits

All circuits include three parts:

  1. Power source (like a battery or solar panel)
  2. Load, also known as a resistor (like a light or motor)
  3. Path (wire or another conductive material)

There are endless ways that circuits can be created and laid out with those three parts. The two main ways that circuits can be laid out, or configured, are series and parallel.

Series Circuit

A series circuit is a circuit that is closed but contains more than one resistor (more than one light bulb, motor, buzzer). When electricity flows through a series circuit the current only travels on one path. If there is a break in the circuit it stops the whole circuit from working. Series circuits also affect the brightness of the bulb, the more resistors you add (LED bulbs) the farther the current has to travel and the LED bulbs become dimmer. 

Parallel Circuit 

A parallel circuit is a circuit that is closed ad like a series circuit it also contains more than one resistor (more than one light bulb, motor, buzzer). However, when electricity flows through a parallel circuit the current goes through every component in the circuit and carries the same amount of current to all of the resistors. Parallel circuits do not affect the brightness and an added benefit of parallel circuits is that if one resistor breaks, the others will continue to work because the current has multiple paths it can travel. 

Makey Makey Circuit Board

Makey Makey is a two-sided circuit board that acts like a keyboard and can communicate with your computer. The front of the Makey Makey has 12 inputs. An input is a place on the board where the computer gets information. When you complete a circuit by touching the space input and the Earth input, the computer gets a message that the space button has been pressed. 

The back of the circuit board has more inputs, called pins. White connector wires are used to connect these pins to the circuit board.

Makey Makey and Scratch

Makey Makey has a number of "Plug and Play" apps that you can use with the Makey Makey board, but Scratch lets you create your own games and sound effects. When you create a closed circuit with the Makey Makey board, your computer registers that as a click or as if you were pressing an arrow key or space bar.

You can program the output, which is the response to the input, with Scratch. You decide whether sounds play or the Scratch character moves when a key or button is pressed.

Event Blocks

Scratch uses Event Blocks to tell the computer when to start the program.

Think about it like this. When you knock on your friend's door, they stand up, walk to the door, and open it. The knock on the door is like the Event Block telling your friend to get up and open the door.

In this Scratch example, when the green flag (Event Block) is clicked, it starts the code attached to the Event Block: move 10 steps, start Meow sound, and then repeat "turn 90 degrees and wait 1 second" four times.

Scratch has another event block that lets you choose what will start the program. You can start the program by pressing the space button, up arrow, down arrow, left arrow, right arrow, and letter keys.

You can find space, arrow keys, and some of the letters on the Makey Makey board. You can create a closed circuit with your Makey Makey board that will start the Event Block.
