Important Vocabulary

  • Multiple - a number that is in a number pattern found by skip counting (for example, multiples of 3 are 3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18. This continues as high as you can skip count)
  • Relationship - in math, this tells how operations work together. For example, subtraction is related to addition, and multiplication is related to skip counting
  • Strategy - a plan to meet a goal
  • Quotient - the answer to a division problem
  • Pattern - a repeated design

Carbon Dioxide (CO2)

Greenhouse gases make Earth warm. Too much of them makes Earth too warm and causes global warming. Carbon dioxide (CO2) is one greenhouse gas that is causing global warming.

We have more CO2 in the atmosphere because we are burning fossil fuels for energy. Burning fossil fuels puts CO2 into the atmosphere. We burn fossil fuels when we drive gas-powered cars and trucks. We also burn fossil fuels when we use energy made from coal, natural oil, or natural gas. The energy that powers many homes and businesses comes from fossil fuels. To slow down global warming, we need to stop burning so much fossil fuel.

Instead of using fossil fuels, we can make electricity from sunlight and wind. Making electricity from sunlight or wind does NOT make CO2. That is why energy that comes from sunlight and wind is called clean energy. Can you think of another clean energy source?

Some things help take CO2 out of the atmosphere. These are called CO2 sinks. Green plants are sinks because they have chlorophyll, so they go through photosynthesis.

 NASA’s Climate Kids

Some of the best information about global climate change that you could find today is on NASA’s Climate Kids Website. The information you find comes directly from NASA’s study of Earth. The site includes articles, activities, interviews, games, and videos. There are games, activities, articles, interviews, and image galleries for every topic on the site: weather & climate, atmosphere, water, energy, and plants & animals.

Climate Kids’ Big Questions

As you review games and activities, it will help if you know about the topics being addressed. Here are the “Big Questions” from NASA’s Climate Kids Website and what you can find in each collection of articles. 

What is global climate change?

What is global climate change? Learn about climate vs. weather, why we should care if Earth is getting warmer, and the cause of global warming.

What is the big deal with carbon?

What is the big deal with carbon? Learn about fossil fuels, how carbon gets into and out of living things, and whether carbon is good or bad.

What is the greenhouse effect?

What is the greenhouse effect? Learn what is a greenhouse, how Earth  is a greenhouse, why planting trees alone does not solve the problem of carbon dioxide (CO2), and the effects of clouds on Earth cooling and warming.

How do we know the climate is changing? 

How do we know the climate is changing?  Learn why Earth is getting warmer, how we know about Earth’s climate from long ago, how such a small temperature change can lead to so much melting, the problem with rising sea levels, and how climate change affects other species.

What is happening in the ocean?

What is happening in the ocean? Learn why the ocean is important, how it soaks up energy and CO2, how it affects climate, and what salt in the ocean does.

What can we do to help?

What can we do to help? Learn what you can do to help nature help us, how to reduce your “carbon footprint” and trash pile, why you should save water, and other ways to make a difference.

 Strategies for Learning Multiplication Facts

Some multiplication facts are easy to remember. The 2s, 3s, 4s, and 5s are the easiest. Look at the chart below. The red line shows all the facts that are the number times itself. These are also easy to remember. The 9s are easy. If you have not yet learned how to use your hands to practice the 9s, watch this video.

The facts that can be hard to remember are the 6s, 7s, and 8s.  There are a total of twelve of these hard ones. But look at the table again. There are really only six! Remember that 7 x 6 is the same as 6 x 7. If you forget, just change the order.

A way to remember the 6s is to double the 3s. A way to remember the 8s is to double the 4s. 

For example, 6 x 7 is the same as 3 x 7 doubled, or 2 x (3 x 7).  8 x 7 is the same as 4 x 7 doubled, or 2 x (4 x 7).

Career Connection and Real-World Application 

Environmental Protection Agency

The Environmental Protection Agency, or EPA, is part of the U.S. government. The EPA has many scientists who collect and study samples from the air, water, and land. They try to make sense of the data they collect. These scientists use math to decide if changes follow a pattern. Some people have the job of telling how important it is to protect our environment. The EPA even has economists who think about the cost of pollution for healthcare, buildings, and jobs. They use many math strategies when they analyze data.

Satellite Engineer

Satellite Engineers are the people who keep the satellites in the sky and working. The engineers use math to calculate weight and the force needed to get that much weight into the air. They design satellites that allow us to use cell phones and the internet. They also design satellites that collect important data on the weather and climate.
