Important Vocabulary

  • Angle - two rays that share an endpoint
  • Right Angle - an angle measuring exactly 90°
  • Acute Angle - an angle measuring less than 90°
  • Obtuse Angle - an angle measuring more than 90° but less than 180°
  • Degrees - the unit of measure for angles
  • Horizontal - parallel to the horizon, or from side to side

What Is Astronomy?

Astronomy is the study of the universe, its objects, and how they move. Astronomers observe space to learn about the past, present, and future of the universe. They also observe space to understand how the universe works. This includes looking at things like planets and moons.

Solar System Planets

A planet is a round object that travels around (or orbits) a star. Planets come in many sizes and are made up of many different materials. Each planet can also have one or more moons nearby. Moons are small rocky bodies. They orbit planets the way planets orbit stars. 

There are eight planets orbiting our own star, the Sun. These objects make up our solar system.

Mercury

Mercury is the closest and smallest planet in our solar system. It is a rocky planet with very little air (or atmosphere) on its surface. It has no moons, and is actually just a little bigger than our own moon.

Venus

Venus is known as Earth's "sister planet." It is about the same size as Earth. It is the second closest planet to the Sun.

Venus has a very thick atmosphere that traps heat from the Sun; this makes it the hottest planet in our solar system.

Like Mercury, Venus has no moons.

Earth

Next is our home planet, Earth. Earth is the only planet we know of that supports life. The reason Earth can support life is because it has the right atmosphere and temperature, and has plenty of water.

Earth has one moon.

Mars

Mars is the last rocky planet of our solar system. It may have once had liquid water on its surface, but today it is dry with a very thin atmosphere. 

Because of its red surface, Mars is also known as the "Red Planet."

Jupiter

Past the inner rocky planets, we find the "gas giants." Jupiter is the closest gas giant and the largest planet orbiting the Sun. Jupiter is as wide as eleven Earths. It has no known rocky surface—only many layers of swirling gas. 

Jupiter has at least 53 moons.

Saturn

This gas giant is famous for its large rings. It is slightly smaller than Jupiter and is also made up of layers of gas.

Saturn also has many moons. We know of 62 so far.

Uranus

Uranus is the first of the "ice giants" at the far edge of our solar system. It is incredibly cold because it is so far from the Sun. It has a very heavy liquid and gas surface covering a rocky center. 

Uranus has 27 known moons and is famous for its unusual sideways spin.

Neptune

The last and farthest planet in our solar system is Neptune. It is an ice giant similar in size to Uranus. It also has many liquids and gases around a small rocky center.

Neptune has 13 known moons.

How Is a Protractor Used?

A protractor is the tool that is used to measure angles. A protractor measures in degrees. 

To measure an angle, put the endpoint of the angle in the center point of the protractor. One ray should always go through the 0° point on the protractor. The place where the other ray crosses is the measure of your angle. Many protractors have two sets of numbers and you have to decide which value to choose. How to Draw an Angle

To draw an angle of a certain measure, start with a horizontal line. Add a point to the line that will be the endpoint for the angle. Place the center of the protractor on the endpoint and be sure the end of your horizontal line goes through 0°. Make a small mark at the edge of the protractor at the measure you were given. Connect the endpoint to the mark you made.

What is a Benchmark Angle?

A benchmark angle is an angle that is easy to recognize without measuring. The most well known benchmark angle is a right angle, which has a measure of 90°. A right angle looks like the corner of a square or rectangle.

An angle with a measure of 180° is another benchmark angle. This angle forms a straight line. 

Career Connection and Real-World Application

Angles and Distances in Careers

It is important for travel guides and civil engineers to know specific directions between locations. The precise measurement of angles is used in jobs such as mechanical engineers, architects, and surveyors. Civil engineers design, study, and build systems such as roadways, tunnels, bridges, and pipe systems for moving water. Surveyors take close measurements of Earth's surface for construction projects and use angles to determine distances, especially distances that are hard to measure. A land surveyor uses instruments that help calculate the distances and measure the angle of elevation, or how much the land slopes up or down.
