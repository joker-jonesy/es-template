Important Vocabulary

  • Hypothesis - an educated guess, or a guess you make based on information you already know.
  • Motion - the action or process of moving or being moved.
  • Evaluating - to determine the significance, worth, or condition of, usually by careful appraisal and study.
  • Inertia - do nothing or to remain unchanged.
  • Mass - the amount of matter or substance that makes up an object.
  • Weight - the force with which an object near the Earth is attracted toward the center of the body by gravity.
  • Momentum - describes the strength of a moving object.
  • Friction - force that resists the sliding or rolling of one solid object over another.
  • Velocity - measure of how fast something moves in a particular direction.
  • Position - the way in which something or someone is placed or arranged.
  • Acceleration - measurement of change in an object's speed and direction.

Motion

Everything that happens in the solar system, even here on Earth has an effect on every other thing. Physics is the study of those effects. Physics is a science that relates to all other sciences: chemistry, biology, geology, astronomy, meteorology, engineering, etc. If you can name it, chances are physics is involved.

The physics of motion is all about force. A force is needed to make an object move, or to change its motion. Changes in motion don't just happen on their own. How fast an object moves is called its speed or velocity, and it can be influenced by forces.

Acceleration is a twist on the idea of velocity. Acceleration is a measure of how much the velocity of an object changes in a certain time. Velocity can either increase or decrease over time. Mass is another big idea in motion. Mass is the amount of something there is. This is not to get confused with weight. Mass is how much area that object takes up, while weight is how much force that object is pushing against gravity.

How Speed Affects Motion

Forces affect how objects move. They may cause the motion; they may also slow, stop, or change the direction of motion of an object that is already moving. An object with more weight is more massive, and an object that is massive has more inertia. Inertia is what causes an object to resist change in speed.

Hypotheses: What Are They and Why Do We Use Them?

A hypothesis is a guess as to why or how something has or will happen. A hypothesis can be tested to figure out if it’s correct. We use our background knowledge and what we know about the world to make this guess. While many believe that we only use hypotheses only in science, we actually use them in our daily lives. For example, you or a parent may be cooking and think, “What ingredient should I add to make this taste better? Hmm, maybe garlic powder!” You are forming a hypothesis before testing it out, likely based on food you’ve made or had in the past.

When scientists develop a hypothesis, they normally follow these steps:

  1. Make observations.
  2. Define the problem or question.
  3. Research the problem.
  4. Use the research and observations to form a hypothesis, which is the solution or answer to the problem or question.

When you make a hypothesis, ask yourself:

  • What is your question or problem?
  • Have you observed a situation like this before? What did you notice?
  • How could you learn more before making your hypothesis? What would you need to know?
  • What is your answer or solution? Remember: It should make sense with what you’ve observed or researched!

Line graphs can be used to show how something changes over time. Line graphs are good for showing data that was collected in a short time period. Scaled line graphs have specific information that is important to include.

The Title

The title offers a short explanation of what is in your graph. This helps the reader identify what they are about to look at. It can be creative or simple as long as it tells what is in the graph. The title of the graph below is Hospital Admittees.

The Legend

The legend tells what each line represents. Just like on a map, the legend helps the reader understand what they are looking at. The legend for this graph shows that the pink line is used for females and the blue line is used for males.

Y-axis

In line graphs, the y-axis runs vertically (up and down). Typically, the y-axis has numbers for the amount of stuff being measured. The y-axis usually starts counting at 0 and can be divided into as many equal parts as you want to. The y-axis starts at 0 and increases by 2.

The Data

The most important part of your graph is the information, or data, it contains. Line graphs can present more than one group of data at a time.

X-axis

In line graphs, like the one above, the x-axis runs horizontally (flat). Typically, the x-axis has numbers representing different time periods or names of things being compared. The x-axis represents the days of the week.

Career Connection and Real-World Application 

Roller Coaster Engineer

Have you ever been on a roller coaster? A wooden roller coaster that whips you around, a steel roller coaster that twists and turns you upside down? Engineers who design roller coasters use a lot of physics in order to make a ride that is not only fun, but safe. They have to think about how much all of the materials weigh, plus the weight of all of the people who can ride at once, then the speed to allow it to make it over the drops.

Semi-Truck Driver

Truck drivers carry heavy loads all over the county. They are needed to transport goods from farms and distribution centers to the rest of the nation. After they get their load, they get sent to a certain location. Due to their size and the weight of their load, they have to adjust their speed, find different roads, and even find different ways to turn and move around objects.
