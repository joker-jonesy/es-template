NASA's Climate Kids

Some of the best information about global climate change that you could find today is on NASA's Climate Kids website. 

The information you find comes directly from NASA's study of Earth. The site includes articles, activities, interviews, games, and videos. You can focus your research on weather & climate, atmosphere, water, energy, plants & animals, or big questions.  

Making Convincing Presentations

What makes a convincing presentation?

Click on each of the topics below to find a few tips.

Main Message

  • Decide what you want to tell people. What is the main message you want people to know, think, or do after your presentation?
    Examples:
    • We want people to know that global climate change is real and that there is proof.
    • We want people to think it is important to help prevent global climate change.
  • Once you have your main message, make sure everything you say supports the message.

Outline

Outline your presentation. This helps make sure you cover everything you need in an order that makes sense. Here is an example outline you can use:

  • Share your main message.
    Example: Global climate change is a real problem and it affects everyone, even lemurs in Madagascar.
  • Give background information. What do people need to know in order to understand your message?
    Examples: What is climate? What is global climate change? What are greenhouse gases?
  • Restate your message and share proof.
    Example: How do we know that global climate change is a real problem? We can see the effect it has on lemurs in Madagascar. Here is what's going on...
  • Share the opposite opinion. What are the arguments?
    Example: Some people don't believe in global climate change because it can still be very cold at times. They say... [share specific arguments].
  • Explain why you disagree with these opinions and arguments.
    Example: If we look at the evidence, then we see that having periods of cold weather does not change the fact that the global climate is getting warmer. Here is a chart that shows the average temperatures over time...
  • Share what people can do, now that they got your message.
    Example: Even though global climate change is happening, we can still make a difference. Here are things you can do to help. You can carpool or ride a bike. This helps because... [share the reasons].

Design

Show pictures, videos, charts, maps, or any other visuals that will help make your point.

  • Before choosing your visuals, ask yourself, "does this support my main message?" If it does, then it is worth showing in your presentation. If it is just a related picture that does not prove your point, then it may be better to leave it out.
  • You may want to present your visuals in a PowerPoint slideshow (or another presentation software).

Communicating

  • Make eye contact with your audience. Look at different people to make sure everyone feels like you are speaking to them.
  • When you get to a really important point, make it sound important with your voice. You can speak with a more excited tone, speak louder, or change your tone in another way to bring attention to what you are saying.
  • Use hand gestures and body language to help make key points.
  • Start and end by stating your main point.
    Example:
    Opening: I am going to prove to you that global climate change is real, and that we have the power to slow it down.
    Closing: Now that I have shown you proof that global climate change is really happening, let's do what we can to help!

Practice and Improve

  • You do not have to memorize your presentation word for word. If you remember your key examples and points, then you can talk more comfortably about your message.
  • Practice in front of a friend and ask for tips.
