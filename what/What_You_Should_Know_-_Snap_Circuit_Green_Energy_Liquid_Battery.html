Important Vocabulary

  • Decimal - a number representing a whole and part of a whole separated by a decimal point
  • Tenth - the first digit to the right of a decimal point
  • Hundredth - the second digit to the right of a decimal point
  • Thousandth - the third digit to the right of a decimal point
  • Round - the nearest value of a number based on place value to the right of the number
  • Place Value - the numerical value of a digit based on the location in a number

What Types of Energy Do You Use?

Each day you use energy. You use it to go to school, to play, to do chores, and even to grow! What types of energy do you use?

Electricity powers lights, computers, appliances and more.

Light from the Sun is energy that helps us see and keeps us warm.

Food is energy. The food you eat gives you the energy you need to learn, play, and grow.

We burn oil, gas, and coal to run our cars, heat our houses, and make electricity.

What is Renewable Energy?

Renewable energy sources include wind, solar, water, biomass, and human power.

Wind turbines are turned by the wind. As the turbines turn, they spin a generator. The motion creates energy, which converts into electricity.

Sunlight shines on solar panels, which are made to convert light energy into electricity.

Flowing water turns a wheel to do work or to make electricity.

Energy made from plants and other organic materials.

People do work to create energy.

Alternative Energy and Electricity

Renewable energy is also called alternative energy since it is an alternative to fossil fuels like oil and coal.

Electricity is a very important part of most alternative energy systems. We cannot plug tools into renewable sources directly. In many cases, we need to convert the energy into electricity.

One way to make electricity is to use a force, such as wind, water, or human power, to turn a generator. A generator is like a motor but instead of electricity making the motor spin, the spinning generator creates electricity.

Wind Power: Wind turns the blades, which spins the generator. The motion of the generator creates energy, which converts to electricity.

Human Power: Pedaling the bicycle spins the wheel, which turns the generator. This converts the energy into electricity, which powers the blender.

The electricity produced can be used directly by a device while it is being generated. However, we often want to store the energy and use it at a more convenient time.

For example, solar panels are a source of electricity during the day when the sun is out, but electricity cannot be generated at night. The same challenge is seen with wind turbines on days when there is no wind. 

How can we use these inconsistent energy sources to power our devices any time we need electricity?

What Is Electricity?

Electricity is usable energy. All of the objects around us are made up of small particles called atoms. Each atom is made up of even smaller particles, known as electrons, which have a negative (-) charge, and protons, which have a positive (+) charge. 

Think of charge as the relationship between particles: Opposite charges attract (move towards) each other and similar charges oppose (move away from) each other. 

When many electrons (-) at one location move towards many protons (+) at another location, we call this an electric current, or a flow of electrons. The general term for current and charge interactions is known as electricity.

Voltage is like the "muscle" that pushes electric energy to flow. Voltage is measured in volts (V).

Fewer volts is like less muscle. The electrons move more slowly.

More volts is like tons of muscle. The electrons move more quickly.

An extreme example of an electric current can be seen during many thunderstorms. Lighting is simply electrons moving from the negatively charged clouds to the more positively charged ground.

Making a Pathway for Electricity

Even though lightning is an electric current, it would be very hard to use it to generate usable electricity. Lightning comes in random bursts and we never know where exactly it will land. Instead, we need to control the path that the electrons travel. We do this by creating circuits, or closed pathways, connecting negatively (-) and positively (+) charged particle sources.

Electrons cannot move easily through every type of material. In fact, electrons will flow through the most conductive material that they touch. Most conductors are metal (like copper, silver, and aluminum). We can use conductive wires to transport electrical energy between locations. 

For example, copper wiring is used in many homes to move electricity to all the rooms.

Storing Electricity

To store energy that we produce, we use batteries.

Batteries of all sizes and shapes store electricity. A rechargeable battery is able to collect energy from an energy source and then release that same energy any time we need it. Solar panels and turbines change renewable energy into electricity. The electricity charges batteries and the batteries store the electricity.

Snap Circuits Liquid Battery

What does the Snap Circuit liquid battery have in common with all batteries?

It has cells. In this battery, the cells hold liquids like water, soda, or juice. In other batteries, the cells contain special chemicals.

The battery has electrodes. Electricity is conducted between two electrodes. The electrodes in your battery are zinc and copper, and they provide connection points that link the liquid source with the electrical circuit. They are two different materials because each electrode reacts differently with a given liquid, one creating a positive charge around it while the other creates a negative charge. This is what drives your battery.

NOTE: Though your liquid battery is not a rechargeable battery, it is very useful for showing how any battery is able to release its stored energy in order to create a useful circuit.

Rounding to the Nearest Hundredth of a Second

Rounding is used to simplify a number but keep it close to the original value. Money is a relevant example of rounding numbers. Money ends in the hundredths place value. When adding, subtracting, multiplying, or dividing the cost of an item, it may be necessary to round to the nearest hundredths place.

There are a number of other uses for rounding including estimating cost, height, length, taxes, summarizing data, etc. 

Below you will find examples of rounding to the nearest hundredth of a second. The bold number represents the value we look at to determine if the place value to the left should remain the same or be increased to the next digit. The underlined number is the actual number that will end the new number once we round.

If the number you are rounding is followed by a 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4, round the number down. If the number you are rounding is followed by a 5, 6, 7, 8, or 9, round the number up.
