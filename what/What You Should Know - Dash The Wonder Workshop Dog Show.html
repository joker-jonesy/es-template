Important Vocabulary

  • Stacks - a group of commands arranged in layers
  • Event - an action that causes something to happen
  • Event-driven Program - a program designed to run blocks of code or functions in response to specified events (example: a mouse click)
  • Event Handling - a term for the coding tasks involved in making a program respond to events by triggering functions
  • Expression - a math sentence with a minimum of one math operation and a minimum of two numbers
  • Numerical Expression - a combination of numbers and operations
  • Variable Expression - a combination of numbers, variables, and operations
  • Operations - addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division
  • Variable - a symbol used to represent an unknown value, usually represented using a letter of the alphabet

How Is Programming a Robot Similar to Training a Puppy?

Let’s say you have a new puppy. Puppies need training, so they will listen when you tell them to do something. Just like babies, dogs do not know what words mean until you teach them. When you say “sit,” you have to teach your dog what you want it to do.

When you write a program, you are teaching a robot what to do. You, like a new dog owner, are giving a set of commands to your computer.

You expect the robot to perform actions or tasks after giving it commands. But instead of speaking and using treats to encourage your dog, you will write instructions in a language that your robot understands.

Unlike a dog, the computer always listens if the command is given correctly.

How Do We Control Robots?

Robots need step-by-step instructions to do a job. They can only do what humans program them to do. A program tells a robot what to do, when to do it, and under what conditions. Conditionals are instructions that depend on whether something is true or false. Imagine we're writing a program to help us decide what to wear outside each day. If the temperature is at or below freezing, the program should warn us to wear a snow jacket.

What Is Code?

Computer code is a set of instructions that tells a computer what to do. Each block of code is a single instruction or command.

Computers and electronics with computers in them seem smart, but this is only because a human, like a computer programmer, software engineer, or developer, wrote a set of instructions for them.

DRIVE Blocks

DRIVE blocks control where and how Dash moves. Forward and backward blocks use centimeters as the units to measure how far it goes. To make Dash turn, you will use degrees as the units to measure how far it should turn.

How many centimeters are in an inch? Find out and use this to help you estimate the distance in inches.

LOOK Blocks

LOOK blocks make Dash’s head move any way you want. The LOOK blocks use degrees as units of measurement. You can change the number of degrees to turn Dash’s head to the left or right.

Try programming your Dash to turn 360 degrees. What happens? What about 90 degrees? 180 degrees? Use a protractor tool to find the exact angle you want Dash to turn.

Sensors

Sensors collect this information called input. Robot sensors gather information from the surroundings and send it to the computer brick (or robot’s brain). Robot sensors can only be used if the robot’s program asks for information from them! Similarly, the robot can only act on information from the sensors if its program tells it to do so! The sensors send information through the wires (similar to the nervous system in the human body) that connect to the computer brick, which uses the information if its program requires it.

Expressions

Expressions are math sentences with a minimum of one math operation and a minimum of two numbers. There are two main types of mathematical expressions:

A numerical expression is a combination of numbers and operations. Operations include addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.

(5+8)÷2

A variable expression is a combination of numbers, variables, and operations. Variables are symbols used to represent an unknown value, usually represented using a letter of the alphabet.

(5+x)⋅7

You will notice that these expressions do not have an equal sign (=). An equal sign would make these equations. Without an equal sign, these are expressions.

Interpreting Expressions

In this project, you will be asked to write and interpret numerical expressions. Interpreting a numerical expression means that you will find and share meaningful information about the expression. For example, if you consider the expression (18-6)×3, you will see that it is three times larger than (18-6).

Baker

Bakers combine a love of pastries, creativity, and math. Thinking in terms of numerical expressions is particularly useful when increasing a recipe to bake in bulk. When people bake muffins for themselves or their families, they probably make one batch and are able to follow the recipe as it is written. However, bakers need to bake enough to meet the needs of their customers, so they need to know how to easily multiply a recipe. Numerical expressions help. If a baker is supplying six dozen muffins for a holiday party and her recipe is for one dozen, she will need to multiply each ingredient by six. Let’s say the recipe calls for 1 cup of flour, ⅓ cup sugar, 1 egg, and ½ cup butter. The baker could think in terms of the numerical expression 6 × (1 + ⅓ + 1 + ½) and recognize that she needs six eggs, six ⅓ cups of sugar, etc.
