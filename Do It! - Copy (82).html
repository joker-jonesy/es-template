Do It!

Your challenge is to design, build, and test the best-insulated cup possible. 

Using the Go!Temp Sensor

The experiments in this Liftoff Challenge use the Vernier Go!Temp sensor.

Go!Temp is an electronic temperature sensor that plugs into your computer.

If you have not used this sensor before, it is best to practice with the Go!Temp Sensor Practice Activity

The Best Cup Design Challenge

Do you think you can design the best insulated cup?

You will start with a simple plastic cup and add insulation; you can use materials in the SmartLab or bring supplies from home. 

  1. Start by thinking about insulated cups that you have seen and used. What were they made of? What features do they have in common? 
  2. Consider what made them practical insulators. Maybe they fit comfortably in your hand, were lightweight, or inexpensive. An insulated cup is not very useful if it is too heavy to hold or too bulky to carry around.
  3. Think about what you may already know about insulation in general. What could be good materials for you to use in your design? Metals? Plastics? Fabrics? Consider your past experience with certain cups, clothing, carpets, and other common insulators.

Begin creating a presentation for your project. You could use PowerPoint, Google Slides, or another method of presentation. Write a list summarizing your ideas about what makes a good insulating cup.

Cup With No Insulation

See how well a thin plastic cup of hot water holds its heat without any added insulation. In science, we call this test a control. You can compare this data to your own cup design later to see how much of a difference the insulation made.

  1. Fill your cup with the hottest water you can get from the faucet.
  2. Connect the Go!Temp temperature sensor to your computer; launch Logger Lite.
  3. Open #13 Design Your Own Thermos.
  4. Allow the Go!Temp temperature reading to reach the starting temperature of the water. When the reading stops increasing, begin recording temperature data for 20 minutes.
  5. Subtract the final temperature from the starting temperature to calculate the heat loss.
  6. Click “Store” and label this graph “Control Run”.
  7. Click “Save As” under the File menu; name the file “Best Cup” and save it to your student folder.

Best Cup Design

Now you will design your "best cup" using different insulation materials. You can start with a drawing or begin to experiment with different designs and materials. Use materials you have in the SmartLab or ones you brought from home. Take photographs or scans of your design in progress. In your presentation, include these pictures along with design notes and observations. Do not forget that you are designing a cup; there has to be a way to drink out of it.

  

Test Your Design

  1. Connect the Go!Temp temperature sensor to your computer and launch Logger Lite.
  2. Open the “Best Cup” Logger Lite file you created with your control run.
  3. Use the Predict Tool to make a prediction about how your cup design will compare to the control run.
  4. Fill your “best cup” with the hottest water you can get from the faucet.
  5. Allow the Go!Temp temperature reading to reach the starting temperature of the water. When the reading stops increasing, begin recording temperature data for 20 minutes.
  6. Subtract the final temperature from the starting temperature to calculate the heat loss.

Compare Results

How did your cup design do? Include a picture of your “best cup" in your presentation. Label the parts and materials and describe how your design is meant to work. Include your experimental results in your presentation and discuss your observations. Include screenshots of Logger Lite along with labels and analysis of your findings. 

Answer these questions:

  • How do you think your cup performed? 
  • How did it perform compared to the control run?
  • How did it perform compared to your prediction? 
  • Did it meet your requirements for a good cup design?
  • What was the best feature of your cup design?
  • What could you do to improve your design?

Share your results with your classmates. If you have time, try to improve your design and get better results.
