Energy 

When we want to watch TV or play a video game, we have to make sure our TV or game console is plugged in because it needs electricity to work. Electricity provides energy and we get the electricity we need from many different resources. 

Non-Renewable and Renewable Resources 

A lot of the energy we use comes from non-renewable resources, including coal and natural gas. Non-renewable resources are energy sources that can run out. As the resources get harder to find, people will do almost anything to get them, including things that can harm our environment and people. Once we use up all of these energy sources, we will not be able to get more.

Renewable resources are resources that we can get more of like sun and wind. When we collect renewable resources, there is not usually a negative impact on the environment. We are working hard to use more renewable energy sources to meet our energy needs.

How Much Energy Do We Use in the United States? 

In 2019, the United States had about four percent of the world’s population but we used about 17 percent of the world’s energy resources (source: eia.gov Energy Information Administration). In 2020, this came to an average of 10,715 kilowatt hours (kWh) per year for an American home–893 kWh per month.

These averages include the energy used to heat and cool our homes, wash our clothes, cook our food, and even entertain ourselves with TVs, video games, computers, and other electronic devices.

Factors Affecting Home Energy Use

Climate, Home, and Insulation

While 893 kWh per month is the average energy usage for an American household, the average in each state varies. Differences in climate and the need for heating and/or cooling systems is one major factor. You might think that a person who lives in a cold climate like Alaska will use more energy to heat a home than someone living in Alabama. However, most Alaskan homes have better insulation than a home in Alabama, so the heat tends to stay in the home once it is warmed up. Many Alabamians also use energy to cool their homes in the summer whereas most Alaskan homes don’t need air conditioning. 

The age of homes, home sizes, and how well homes are made also have a huge impact on energy use. Older homes or cheaply-made homes may not be as well insulated or well designed, so it could take more energy to keep these structures cool in the summer and warm in the winter. Larger homes also need more energy to heat and cool the interior buildings even if the insulation is good.

How Many Appliances, How Often They Are Used, and How Energy Efficient?

The amount of energy we use can also depend on how many electronics and appliances we have, and how often we use them. People who could afford larger homes may also be able to afford more appliances. Larger families may also consume more energy, even if they live in a smaller home, since they may need to use appliances like washing machines, dryers, and dishwashers more often.

Families that need to use appliances more often can still save energy by using ones that are energy efficient. Fifty years ago, appliances and electronics used a lot more energy. Back then, a normal light bulb (called an incandescent bulb) used 40 to 100 watts per hour; now we have LED bulbs that use just 10 watts! An old TV screen (called a CRT television) used 90 watts per hour; now a 19 inch LED TV uses 32 watts (which is less than an old light bulb). However, the average home had fewer appliances and electronics; depending on where you lived, a family home may have had only one TV and basic appliances like a refrigerator and freezer. It probably had no central heating or air (systems that heat and/or cool the whole house), which today accounts for almost half of a home’s energy usage.  

Appliances and electronics have become easier to make, more affordable, and there are more energy efficient options. However, the average home now has many more electronic devices like central heating and air, multiple TVs, multiple computers, a dishwasher, microwave, toaster, video game console, laundry machine, and dryer. All of these electronics need energy to work and you may be surprised by how much energy some of these appliances use.

What Is a Kilowatt Hour?

A Kilowatt hour (kWh) is a unit of measurement that tells us how much energy something uses. Despite the name, it doesn’t mean the number of kilowatts you use in one hour. 1 kilowatt hour is the amount of energy used to run a 1,000 watt appliance for an hour. How to Calculate Kilowatt Hours Used in a Day, Week, Month, and Year

Let’s say that you are trying to show how much energy a household uses in a day, a week, a month (30 days), and a year. In 2020, the monthly average for a U.S. household was 893 kWh.       

  1. Divide the monthly average by 30 to calculate the average daily usage. Round the value. For this example, we will round to the nearest thousandth. 89330=29.767 kWh

  2. Using the average daily usage, multiply by 7 to estimate the amount used in one week. Now we will round to the ones place. 29.7677=208 kWh

  3. If you didn’t already know the monthly estimate, you would multiply the daily estimate by 30 to find this value. 29.76730=893 kWh

  4. Multiply the monthly estimate by 12 to calculate the yearly usage. 893x12=10,716 kWh

Career Connection and Real-World Application

Careers in Energy Reduction 

Many people have made it their life’s mission to reduce the amount of energy used worldwide and promote the use of renewable energy. Conservation scientists maintain, improve, and strive to protect their country’s natural resources. Conservation scientists work to preserve the land and encourage others to do the same. Geoscientists present others with details about the structure, composition, and processes of the Earth so we can learn about its past, present, and future. Geoscientists work with others to help improve the impact we have on the Earth to better preserve it for the future
