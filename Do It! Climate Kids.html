Do It! Climate Kids: Sharing Climate and Weather Data

 The Challenge

Your challenge is to learn how patterns are used to report and graph data. Then, you will make and interpret graphs for weather and climate. Finally, you will analyze facts and data on climate change and use your findings to make a presentation to suggest things we can do to help. You need to present the proof and results accurately if you are going to be convincing. Then, make suggestions for what we can do to help.

Graphing Weather and Climate

  1. Prepare to make a weather graph. Find the high and low temperatures for the past week in your area. One website you can use is: Accuweather or Weather.com.
  2. Enter your zip code or city. Then click on Monthly and pick the last seven days from the calendar.
  3. Enter the temperatures into a table like the one below. Or make a file in Google Sheets or Excel and enter the data.
    Day High Temperature Low Temperature

    Saturday
  4. Using the table you just filled out, decide on the type of scaled graph you would like to make. You may want to use a thermometer graphic to represent every 10 degrees. Or, you can use one bar to represent every 10 degrees.
  5. The next step is to make a key. This tells what each bar shows.
  6. Add a title with your location. Then put the timeframe below the graph. For a local weather graph, this would be the month and dates when you collected your data.
  7. Finally, write at least three questions that can be answered by your graph. For example, you can ask how much warmer one day was than another, or what the difference is between the high and low temperature on one day. This will be part of your submission.
  8. Now prepare to make a graph to show the climate where you live. Find the average high and low monthly temperatures for the past year for your area. Go to https://www.usclimatedata.com and type in the name of your city.

Plan Your Presentation

Use Climate Kids website to find the articles, pictures, and other resources needed for your presentation.

  1. Research global climate change. Make sure you understand what it is. Read some of the Big Questions articles. In My Portfolio, list which ones you read and your major takeaway from each. Here are a few recommended articles to choose from:
    1. What is global climate change?
    2. What is the big deal with carbon?
    3. What is the greenhouse effect?
    4. What can we do to help?
  2. Research the proof. How do we know that the climate is changing? Make a list of the resources you review and your major takeaway from each. Here are a few suggestions to choose from:
    1. The Climate Time Machine (You may want to take screenshots of what you see in this interactive activity.) Try to capture screenshots of every 10 years since 1900.
    2. How do we know the climate is changing?
    3. What is happening in the ocean?
    4. What is ocean acidification?
    5. Lemur moms
    6. Birds and climate change
    7. What can trees tell us about climate change?
  3. Choose a topic that interests you. Then, explore some of the other articles, interviews, and games that give you more information on your topic. Again, list the resources you review and your major takeaway from each.
  4. Find pictures, charts, videos, or other multimedia to help prove your point. You can also take screenshots of games or videos.
    HINT: Put “gallery” into the search bar and see what shows up on the Climate Kids website.
  5. Research what we can do to help. You can find ideas in articles, games, interviews, and more.

Prepare Your Presentation

  1. Outline your argument.
  2. Use PowerPoint or another presentation software to organize and show your visuals.
  3. Practice your presentation in front of friends or family. Ask for tips on how to improve.
  4. Present your argument to your class.
