Important Vocabulary

  • Coordinate Pair - an ordered pair of numbers that are used to locate a specific point on the coordinate plane
  • Conductor - material that allows electricity to flow through it
  • Insulator - material that does not allow electricity to flow through it
  • Electricity - a form of energy
  • Circuit - a path for electricity to flow
  • Coordinate Plane - a two-dimensional plane that is formed when two number lines intersect, perpendicular to one another

What Are Conductors?

You might have noticed that electrical devices, like light bulbs and toasters, use metal parts and wires. This is because electricity flows easily through metal. When electricity flows through something, we say it conducts electricity and we call that material a conductor. Did you know that other materials conduct electricity too?

Water Is a Conductor Your Body Is a Conductor

Almost all water has small particles of other materials floating in it that help conduct electricity. One example you see (and probably eat) every day is salt. When you stir salt in water, the salt dissolves into particles too small to see. These tiny particles of salt mix with the water and make it conduct electricity.

Did you know that your body conducts electricity? Your body contains water with particles mixed in. The food you eat and the water you drink mix with liquids in your body. This allows electricity to flow through you. Your body is mostly water. If you weigh 100 pounds, about 65 pounds of that is water.

Electricity will also flow through other things that contain water. Makey Makey works with many materials – like fruit and Play-Doh – because the water they contain conducts electricity.

How Do Computer Controllers Work?

Have you ever wondered how a mouse, keyboard, or game controller works? What do you think they would look like if you took one apart to see inside?

If you look inside a computer keyboard, you would see a board with lots of tiny metal ribbons. When you push a button on the keyboard it connects two ribbons and completes an electrical circuit. It’s like turning on a switch. Every button completes a different circuit in your keyboard. Computer software determines what each button does—like typing the letter “A”, or making a game player jump.

Patterns

Patterns are everywhere. Patterns use and follow rules. Some patterns are numerical; these patterns use numbers. An example of a numerical pattern is 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24. This pattern starts with 0 and adds 4. If you create a second pattern with the rule, “Add 6” and that starts with 0, your pattern would look like this: 0, 6, 12, 18, 24, 30, 36. You can combine both of these patterns on a table to help you compare them.

Pattern 1 0 4 8 12 16 20 24
Pattern 2 0 6 12 18 24 30 36

From the table, you can identify a relationship between the two patterns and make ordered pairs. Let Pattern 1 be the x-coordinate and Pattern 2 be the y-coordinate.

Now you can see the ordered pairs: (0,0), (4,6), (8,12), (12, 18), (16, 24), (20,30), (24,36).

These ordered pairs can then be graphed on a coordinate plane. This allows you to see the patterns and how they relate to each other. You can make observations about how these patterns relate. Comparing these two patterns, the second pattern is increasing at a much faster rate than the first pattern.

Coordinate Planes

A coordinate plane is a two-dimensional plane that is formed when two number lines intersect perpendicularly to one another. The horizontal number line is called the x-axis, and the vertical number line is called the y-axis. The point where they intersect is the origin. Coordinate planes use coordinate pairs to show locations on the plane. The first number is the x-value; this gives the horizontal distance from the origin. The second number is the y-value; this gives the vertical distance from the origin. Coordinate planes can be used to graph different forms of data. Doctors use coordinate planes to track changes in heart rate over time. Explorers and hikers use coordinate planes in the form of maps to find the location of trails, ponds, and other attractions.

In the example shown, to plot (2,4), start at the origin. The first number in the coordinate pair tells you to move 2 units right. The second number in the coordinate pair tells you to move 4 units up.

Career Connection and Real-World Application

Patterns in Careers 

Many jobs use patterns. Textile artisans use several types of patterns. Quilters use repeating visual patterns as they design a quilt top. Their stitches make simple patterns when they sew the quilt top to the backing material as they assemble the quilt. Knitted clothing and blankets follow patterns in the way the yarn is looped together and also in the colors.

Musician

Musicians create music and they use a lot of patterns. The rhythm of their music repeats in a pattern. The words of their songs repeat, making a pattern. Listen to your favorite song and see if you can hear the patterns. Many musicians also utilize circuits as they connect their electrical equipment, including sound systems and instruments.
