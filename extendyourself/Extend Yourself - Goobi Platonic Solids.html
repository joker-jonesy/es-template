Build and Explore 3D Shapes

Three-dimensional shapes are also called 3D or solids. They do not lay flat. The mathematical name for a 3D shape is polyhedron. Some common 3D shapes include cylinders, cubes, and cones. The parts of a 3D shape or polyhedron include:

  • Faces - the polygons that make up the polyhedron.
  • Edges - the lines where faces meet.
  • Vertex or vertices - points on the polyhedron where the edges meet.

Now, turn your polygon into a polyhedron. Remember, a 3D shape does not lay flat like a 2D shape, you can turn it around to see the edges, faces, and vertices.

For example, a rectangle (polygon) can be transformed into a rectangular prism (polyhedron).

A regular prism is not a platonic solid! Why not? What are the characteristics of a platonic solid? (Find out below).

 Plato's Platonic Solids

Plato was a philosopher and mathematician who lived in ancient Greece. He believed that a "perfect" shape meant that all the angles, edges, and faces should be equal. Plato was interested in how many solid (three-dimensional) shapes existed that were "perfect." These shapes became known as the platonic solids.

Plato discovered that there are only five 3D shapes that follow the "perfect rules" of a polyhedron. Closed solids that also have angles and sides that are equal are called polyhedra or platonic solids. The characteristics of a perfect polyhedron or platonic solid:

  • They are made from one type of regular polygon (for example, and the cube is made from squares).
  • All polygons in the shape are the same size.
  • The same number of polygons come together at each point (this means that all of the angles are also equal).

 The Archimedean Solids

The second group of regular polyhedra that doesn't totally follow the rules of platonic solids are called the Archimedean solids and there are thirteen of them. One you might be familiar with is a traditional soccer ball, or truncated icosahedron.

To see examples of more complicated models of this type of symmetry, search online for "Uniform Polychora", which are 4D versions of your 3D Archimedean solids.

 Chance and the Platonic Solids

For many years, people have used the Platonic solids in games of chance. Since all of the faces of the Platonic solids are the same, they make perfect dice with each face having an equal chance of landing upward.

The most common die is the 6-sided cube, but others show up in modern role-playing games and have been used throughout history.
