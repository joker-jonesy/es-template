 Solar Eclipse

When the timing is just right, the Moon will actually move directly between the Sun and the Earth. This predictable event is called a solar eclipse. If you happen to be in the right place at the right time, you will see the moon move across the sun and block out much of its light.

A solar eclipse happened in North America on August 21, 2017 between 10:00 a.m. and 3:00 p.m., depending on the viewing location.

  1. Using the Date and Time settings, was a solar eclipse seen from your location at this time?
  2. Use the fast-forward and rewind tools to watch the eclipse in action.

 Venus Transit

Planets closer to the Sun than the Earth (i.e., Venus and Mars) can move directly between Earth and the Sun, like the moon during a solar eclipse. This event is called a transit. These planets do not block out as much sunlight as a solar eclipse because they are so much further away from Earth than the moon is.

A recent Venus transit happened on June 5, 2012. In North America, it took around six hours to watch Venus move across the Sun—from early afternoon to around sunset, depending on your location.

 Magnitude of Stars

Magnitude is a measure of the brightness of stars. In Stellarium, you can click on different stars to learn information about them, including a value for magnitude.

  1. Collect the magnitude data on at least 10 different stars using Stellarium.
  2. Convert the magnitude data to fractions or mixed numbers. You can do this by reading the decimal using place value. For example, 5.3 can be read as “five and three tenths,” so it can be written as the mixed number 5 3/10.
  3. Use the data to create a line plot of magnitudes using a number line with fractions.
  4. Create a few questions that can be answered about the magnitudes of the stars using the line plot.
