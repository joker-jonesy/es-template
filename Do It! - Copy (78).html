Do It!

Your challenge is to speed up time to see how different objects move across the sky. Use what you learn to draw your own model of our solar system and its motions.

Helpful Tools

Time Controls

The universe is full of motion, but much of it is too slow to see in real time. Stellarium can rewind, pause, and fast-forward time to speed up objects in the sky. Click the fast-forward and rewind buttons more times to speed them up even more. The down arrow button returns you to the current time and date.

Following Objects

   

When you speed up Stellarium using the Center button, you might find that the screen gets "wobbly" when you follow a specific object. To fix this, turn on the Equitorial mount button.

This makes your view follow in the same direction as the celestial sphere's  rotation, so it will smoothly move with the sky.

Celestial Speed-Up

Now use Stellarium to observe and explore how the many objects in the sky travel around the sky.

Celestial Sphere Motion

Get started by seeing how the entire celestial sphere appears to move over time.

  1. Make sure the Centering and the Equatorial mount buttons are OFF to begin. Turn ON the ground.
  2. Start to fast-forward using the Time Controls. What direction does the celestial sphere move? Bonus: What does this tell you about the actual direction the Earth is spinning?
  3. Now turn ON the Equatorial mount button. You are now moving with the celestial sphere, making the ground look like it is spinning instead.
  4. Next, turn the ground OFF and play with the speed some more. Remember—your screen is still moving with the celestial sphere, so the stars should not be moving across the screen.
  5. But, you might see other little dots moving in random directions very quickly between the stars as you fast forward. What are these?
  6. Continue to experiment and get comfortable with the time controls and object motions.

Try slowing down the fast-forward just enough so that you can click on one of these fast-moving objects. What "type" of object does Stellarium say it is?

These objects are artificial satellites—man-made objects sent into orbit around the Earth. This includes things like satellites, space telescopes, the International Space Station, and "space junk" from old satellites or rocket parts.

Solar System Tracking

Now take a closer look at the motions of our solar system's objects.

Step 1: Keep the Equatorial mount button turned ON and Center your view on the Moon or a planet to follow it through the sky.

Step 2: Go to the Sky tab of the Sky and viewing options window and turn on "Planet markers" and "Planet orbits." This will show the path of all of the planets and their moons.

Step 3: Adjust the time speed and watch the object's motion. How does it compare to the background stars?

Step 4: Explore some of the motions of other objects. Can you see the many moons of Jupiter or Saturn orbiting their planet?

Step 5: Go to the Location window and change your "Planet" to Solar System Observer. You are now seeing the entire solar system from an outside location in space!

Step 6: If you want a less messy view, go back to the Sky tab of the Sky and viewing options window and check the "Show planet orbits only" box. 

Step 7: Take a look around and see how each planet and their moons orbit the Sun.

Solar System Sketch

You have watched the motions of our solar system from different views and speeds. Now use what you learned to recreate our solar system on paper or with a computer program of your choice. Go back to Stellarium as needed.

  • Include the Sun, the planets, and some example moons.
  • Draw each object's orbit path.
  • Use arrows to show which direction each object is moving around the Sun/planet compared to other objects.
  • Remember to label everything on your picture.
