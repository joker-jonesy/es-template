To create an enjoyable comic strip, you need:

  • Creativity
  • Characters
  • A sequence of events that communicate a story.

Bill Watterson, the author of Calvin and Hobbes, used both images and words to make a point. He also used humor to tell a story.

Comic Strips Communicate a Sequence of Events

Comic strips follow a sequence of events. The images follow a particular order that helps tell a story.

Next, Susie wants to hold Calvin's stuffed tiger but Calvin insists that Hobbes is a ferocious beast that should not to be handled, no less "squeezed".

Finally, Susie doesn't believe that Hobbes is a ferocious beast, ignores Calvin's dramatic plea, and gives Hobbes a big hug anyway. Calvin gets mad at Hobbes for not being scary and Hobbes tells Calvin that he thinks Susie is cute. What is going on here?

Questions to Consider

  • Does Calvin want to impress Susie by having a big ferocious beast as an imaginary friend?
  • Is he afraid that Susie might not think he is cool if he has a stuffed animal?
  • Is he trying to convince himself that he is cool?
  • In the end, Hobbes was distracted by the cute girl, Susie. Do you think Calvin really likes Susie?
  • What does this story mean to you?

Comic Strips Tell a Story

Calvin and Hobbes comics tell a story of the thoughts, emotions, and actions of an imaginative child. Most people can connect with the innocence of childhood, either from being around kids or being a kid.

Comics are written to connect to their audience, so even if the topic is something out of this world, like witches and monsters, there is always an aspect that the average comic reader can relate to.

  • What do you think the audience for this Broomhilda comic could connect to?
  • Who would enjoy this comic?

Comics are also meant to be funny. They use humor to make a statement. Calvin and Hobbes comics often have a light and cute tone. The reader might be able to relate and it may make the stories even more enjoyable.
