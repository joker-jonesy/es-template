Liquid Battery Power

Your challenge is to design, build, and test electrical circuits that are powered by a liquid battery. 

Can you make electricity from fruit juice, soda, or water? You will learn the answer to this question as you explore how these sources can be used to produce electricity. How could this benefit the environment? Review What You Should Know: What Is Renewable Energy to learn more.

To succeed in your challenge, you probably want to complete some introductory projects first. These experiences will give you the skills needed to build and teach others about your final challenge projects.

For all of your projects, remember to:

  • Record your work
  • Take photographs
  • Label images
  • Write about what you did and what happened

Set Up Instructions

  1. Is your battery assembled? If not, turn to page 4 in the manual for instructions.
  2. Do you have water, salt, juice, and/or soda? To do activities in this challenge, you need these resources.
  3. Locate the jump wires. The liquid battery connects to kit parts with jump wires.
  4. You will need a sink and paper towels to wash and dry the battery when you are done.

   

Tips

  • Gently stir the liquid in the cells about once every 

    minute.
  • Do not drink the liquids used in this experiment!
  • The LEDs are dim. LED bulbs only light up when electricity flows through the circuit in the right direction. Carefully build each circuit following the directions. If the LED does not light up, try turning it around. Use the + sign to help you place your LED correctly.

Introductory Projects

These introductory projects will give you the experience and knowledge needed to succeed in your challenge. You will test how much electricity the liquid power source produces with different types of liquids. Which liquid do you think produces more energy: soda or juice? 

Snap Circuits Green Energy Instruction Manual

  • #62 Liquid Battery - Construct a liquid battery using cola.
  • #63 Juice Battery - Replace the soda in the liquid energy source with fruit juice. Sour tasting juices like lemon or grapefruit work best. Measure the voltage and current for your juice battery like you did with the soda. Try different juices and compare them. 
  • #64 Cola Light - Test LED lights using your cola liquid battery.
  • #65 Yellow Cola - Replace the red LED (D1) with the yellow LED (D5). Compare the LED brightness and voltage change to the red LED in the previous project. The liquid energy source does not produce enough electricity to run the W1 horn or M4 motor. 

  

Advanced Projects

Now you are ready for your final challenge. Complete at least two advanced projects or try creating a unique circuit and running it with your liquid battery.

Salt and other impurities in the water react with the electrodes to produce electricity, just like the cola does in project 64. Which liquid do you think produces more energy: fresh water or salt water?
