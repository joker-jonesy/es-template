Important Vocabulary

  • Work - is done when a force moves an object or load
  • Force - a push or pull on an object that can change the object’s motion or direction
  • Equal sign - a mathematical symbol that means that values on both sides of the equal sign are the same
  • Pan Balance - a type of scale that is a simple lever machine
  • Equation - a number of sentences with an equal sign

Machines Make Work Easier

The job of a machine is to make work easier. It does this by changing how force is used to move an object, or load.

For example, you do work when you push a grocery cart at the store.

Pushing is the force you apply to your cart (your machine) to easily move groceries (your load). You also do work when you pull something in a wagon.

A force can be a push or a pull. 

Simple Machines

There are six types of simple machines. You can learn all about simple machines by watching this video or check out the definitions below. 

Lever

A lever helps lift, pull, or push objects. It is a hard bar or board that rests and rotates on a point, but does not bend. The point (fulcrum) does not move, but it supports the bar and allows it to move.

examples: seesaw, claw of hammer

Wheel and Axle

A wheel connected to a bar called an axle makes moving and turning work easier. 

examples: bicycle wheels, door knobs

Pulley

A pulley can help lift or move a heavy load. It is a wheel with a rope, belt, or chain wrapped around it. With effort from you or a motor pulling at the rope, you can lift things. 

examples: flagpole, construction crane

Inclined Plane

An inclined plane is a ramp or another surface that is higher on one side that eh other. It helps us move heavy things up and down more easily. 

examples: slide, truck ramp

Wedge

A wedge has two flat surfaces (planes) It is wider at one edge and pointer at the other. It is used to push things apart. When you push down on a wedge, it move things away from it. Because of it's shape it can also be used to stop objects from moving like a door and door stop. 

examples: doorstop and ax blade

Screw 

A screw is an inclined plane wrapped in a spiral around a cone or a cylinder.  We often use screws to hold things together. 

examples: screw, jar lid

Simple machines can be used separately, or they can be combined into one machine, called a compound machine. Think about how each of these machines work.

What Does the Equal Sign Mean?

Did you know that many people think that the equal sign means to find the answer to an equation? Actually, when you see an equal sign in an equation, it means that the values on the left and the right of the equal sign are the same. Examine the three equations below to help you visualize the concept.

Another way to read an equation is to use the words “is the same as” when you come to the equal sign. If you have the equation 3 x 2 = 6, instead of saying “three times two equals six,” you can say “three times two is the same as six.”

See if your friends and family can figure out this other way of reading the equal sign. Write down an equation. You can use 5 x 5 = 25 if you cannot think of one. Go ask a friend or family member if they know two different ways to read the equation. When you stump them, tell them the new way that you learned to read the equation.

How Can a Pan Balance Be Used to Find the Weight of Objects?

A pan balance is a type of scale that is a simple lever machine. It has a beam, a fulcrum, and pans on the left and right sides of the beam. Objects are placed in both pans. When the pans are balanced that means that the objects on one side have the same weight/mass as the objects on the other side. It is like an equal sign. The weight of the object in the left pan “is the same as” the weight of the object in the right pan.

In the image below, you will notice that the pans are balanced. This tells us that the clock in the left pan is the same weight as the coins in the right pan.

In this next image, you will notice that the pans are not balanced. This tells us that the book in the left pan is not the same weight as the books in the right pan. Notice that the pan on the right side is slightly lower than the pan on the left side. Do you know what that tells us about the weight of the books? The book in the left pan weighs less than the books on the right.

Parts of a Lever

  • Load - This is the work we want to do. A load is anything that provides resistance to the lever. The elephant is the load.
  • Fulcrum - This is the balance or pivot point for the lever, meaning the lever tilts around it. The green triangle is the fulcrum.
  • Effort - This is where we apply force. If you wanted to lift the load, you would apply effort by pushing down on the lever.

Three Classes of Levers

Select each tab to learn more.

Class 1 Levers Class 2 Levers Class 3 Levers

In a class 1 lever, the fulcrum is between the effort and the load.

Class 1 levers make work easier by reducing the amount of force needed to move a load.

Examples: crowbars and seesaws

       

In a class 2 lever, the load is between the effort and the fulcrum.

Class 2 levers also make work easier by reducing the amount of force needed to move the load.

Examples: wheelbarrows and doors

             

In a class 3 lever, the effort is between the fulcrum and the load.

Class 3 levers actually need more force than it takes to just pick up the load, but they increase the distance and speed of the load's movement.

Examples: brooms and baseball bats
