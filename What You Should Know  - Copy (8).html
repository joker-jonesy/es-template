What You Should Know

What Types of Energy Do You Use? 

You use energy every day. You use it to go to school, to play, to do chores, and even to grow! What types of energy do you use? 

The energy you use to heat your home, cook your food, and turn on your computer is called secondary energy. 

To make secondary energy, we need raw fuel, which is called primary energy.

  

Stored Energy vs. Direct Energy 

Direct energy is used when something is powered straight from a primary energy source. For example, a light powered by a wind turbine gets its direct energy from the wind. In this case, energy only flows when the wind is blowing. But what if we need energy when there is no wind? 

                      

Stored energy is important because it allows us to use secondary energy whenever we need it, not just when a primary energy source is available. For example, we can connect a rechargeable battery to our wind turbine so that it can charge while the wind is blowing. When the wind stops, the energy stored in the battery can now be used. 

Fossil Fuels: Buried Energy

Oil, gas, and coal are fossil fuels. These are non-renewable energy sources because we cannot make them as fast as we use them. Plus, many fossil fuels cause pollution, so there are many reasons why we don't want to rely on fossil fuels.

Alternative Energy and Electricity

Renewable energy is also called alternative energy since it is an alternative to fossil fuels. 

Electricity is a very important part of most alternative energy systems.

We cannot plug tools into renewable sources directly. In many cases, we need to convert the energy into electricity.

Click here to learn more about electricity.

Circuitry: Moving Electricity

To use electricity, we must move it from the source of the renewable energy (like a bicycle or a solar panel) to where we need it (like a blender or your home).

Circuits are the way we move electricity. They are electrical systems that allow us to produce, move, store, and control electricity. In a circuit, electricity flows from a power source (like a solar panel or battery) through a wire path to an electrical device (like a light or motor) and then back to the power source through another wire.

Changing Light into Electricity

The solar cell in your kit changes light energy into electricity. Light is made of particles called photons. Photons move very fast. The photons bump into particles in the solar cell causing them to move. The moving particles create a current of electricity.

When you hear “solar energy,” people are usually talking about energy from the sun that is generated through photovoltaics.

Storing Electricity

We use electricity all day, every day. Where does electricity come from at night if we rely on solar panels?  We need a way to store the electricity produced by renewable energy sources. 

Batteries of all sizes and shapes store electricity. Solar panels and turbines change renewable energy into electricity. The electricity charges batteries and the batteries store the electricity until it is needed.

Reading an Energy Meter

Voltage: 

Voltage is the amount of “muscle” that pushes electric energy to flow. Voltage is measured in volts (V).

Current: 

Current tells us how many electrons flow through the energy meter per second. Current is measured in amps (A).  

Energy: 

A joule (J) is the unit that measures energy. More joules means there is more energy available to do work.

Power:  

A watt (W) is the unit that measures power, which is the amount of energy being used or stored over time. One watt is the same as one joule (energy) per second. More watts means more energy is being generated or stored every second. Power is related to voltage and current.
